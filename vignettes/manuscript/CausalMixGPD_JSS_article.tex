\title{\pkg{CausalMixGPD}: Bayesian Nonparametric Modeling with Optional Heavy-Tail Augmentation and Causal Extensions}
\Plainauthor{Arnab Aich}
\Plaintitle{CausalMixGPD: Bayesian Conditional Dirichlet Process Mixtures with Generalized Pareto Tails and Causal Extensions}
\Shorttitle{\pkg{CausalMixGPD}}

\Abstract{
Heavy-tailed outcomes arise frequently in settings where rare extreme
events drive risk, cost, or policy decisions.  Parametric models often
provide adequate descriptions of the distributional center but can
mischaracterize extreme behavior, whereas fully nonparametric
approaches lack principled mechanisms for tail extrapolation.  This
article introduces \pkg{CausalMixGPD}, an \textsf{R} package for
Bayesian modeling of heavy-tailed outcomes that splices Dirichlet
process mixture models for the bulk of the distribution with optional
generalized Pareto tails within a unified inferential framework.  The
package accommodates both unconditional and covariate-dependent mixture
specifications, performs posterior computation via Markov chain Monte
Carlo through \pkg{nimble}, and extends naturally to arm-specific
outcome models for causal inference.  Posterior predictive summaries
are provided for densities, quantiles, means, survival curves, and
causal contrasts, with particular emphasis on extreme quantiles and
other tail-sensitive functionals.  The same modeling framework
additionally supports predictor-dependent clustering under multiple
kernel families.  Taken together, \pkg{CausalMixGPD} furnishes a
practical and extensible workflow for heavy-tailed density estimation,
model-based clustering, and distributional causal reporting.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Causal inference is conventionally summarized through average treatment
effects, yet many substantive applications demand distributional
comparisons between treatment regimes.  In clinical trials, for
instance, investigators may wish to characterize how a treatment alters
outcomes across the entire outcome scale, including effects among
individuals in the upper tail where events are rare but consequences
can be severe.  This shift from mean-based to distribution-based causal
questions ties the credibility of causal analyses directly to the
quality of the underlying outcome distribution estimates under
competing treatment assignments.

A growing \textsf{R} ecosystem supports Bayesian causal inference
through flexible outcome modeling and posterior uncertainty
quantification, including tree-based and model-averaging approaches
such as
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=bcf}{\textsf{bcf}},
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=bartCause}{\textsf{bartCause}},
and
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=BCEE}{\textsf{BCEE}}
\citep{bcf_pkg,bartCause_pkg,BCEE_pkg}.  While these tools are
effective for mean and heterogeneous effect estimation, distributional
reporting poses additional challenges: quantiles estimated
independently can cross, and tail-focused contrasts require the full
outcome distribution rather than a collection of marginal summaries.
\pkg{CausalMixGPD} addresses these challenges by modeling outcome
densities directly and deriving quantiles, means, survival curves, and
tail summaries from a single posterior predictive object.

Dirichlet process mixtures (DPM) offer a versatile nonparametric
framework for density estimation, as they can accommodate multimodality
and latent heterogeneity without prespecifying the number of mixing
components \citep{EscobarWest1995}.  Within the \textsf{R} ecosystem,
Bayesian nonparametric mixture modeling is well supported by several
packages based on Dirichlet process ideas.
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=dirichletprocess}{\textsf{dirichletprocess}}
provides a flexible interface for specifying DPMs and fitting them by
posterior simulation, yielding inference for mixture weights, component
labels, and predictive densities under user-chosen kernels
\citep{dirichletprocess_pkg}.
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=DPpackage}{\textsf{DPpackage}}
offers a broad suite of Bayesian semi- and nonparametric models,
including DP-based formulations for marginal and conditional densities,
regression functions, and model-based predictive distributions
\citep{jara2011DPpackage}.  More recently,
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=BNPmix}{\textsf{BNPmix}}
emphasizes practical Bayesian nonparametric mixture approaches,
supporting posterior inference for clustering structure and
density/regression estimation under DP and Pitman--Yor-type mixture
specifications \citep{canale2021BNPmix}.

The clustering perspective is also central to the present package.
Because DP mixtures induce random partitions through latent allocation
variables, they naturally support model-based subgroup discovery in
addition to density estimation.  Bayesian cluster analysis is typically
reported through label-invariant summaries such as pairwise
co-clustering probabilities \citep{Binder1978} and representative
partitions selected via Dahl's least-squares criterion
\citep{Dahl2006}.  In regression settings, clustering itself can depend
on predictors: dependent Dirichlet process constructions allow
covariates to modulate mixture weights, component-specific parameters,
or both
\citep{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000,DeIorio2004,Quintana2022}, and
predictor-dependent stick-breaking schemes provide a practical route
for covariate-modulated cluster prevalence \citep{Ren2011}.  Existing
software supports portions of this workflow but typically focuses on
bulk density estimation and clustering under a single kernel family
without explicit accommodation for heavy-tailed data.  Consequently,
applications requiring both latent subgroup discovery and tail
extrapolation still necessitate additional modeling outside the
standard Bayesian nonparametric mixture workflow.

Estimating quantities far into the tail demands special care when data
become scarce, and fitting a single model to heavy-tailed data can
distort the fit in the central region.  Extreme value theory (EVT)
provides a principled framework for tail modeling and extrapolation.
EVT characterizes the asymptotic distribution of threshold
exceedances, enabling estimation of tail probabilities and quantiles
beyond the range of observed data
\citep{BalkemaDeHaan1974,Pickands1975,Coles2001}.  Bayesian
implementations of EVT are available in \textsf{R} for settings
requiring posterior uncertainty quantification of tail quantities.
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=evdbayes}{\textsf{evdbayes}}
provides Bayesian inference for standard extreme-value models via
posterior simulation, enabling posterior and posterior-predictive
summaries for extreme-event quantities \citep{evdbayes_pkg}.  For
peaks-over-threshold (POT) approaches,
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=POT}{\textsf{POT}} implements
a focused collection of tools for GPD modeling and threshold-based tail
analysis \citep{POT_pkg}.  For extreme quantile estimation and
rare-event tail summaries,
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=extremefit}{\textsf{extremefit}}
provides a dedicated software implementation
\citep{durrieu2018extremefit}.  These existing tools typically
emphasize either flexible bulk modeling or dedicated tail analysis;
applications requiring both predictor-dependent clustering and causal
contrasts in the upper tail still oblige users to integrate multiple
disparate workflows.

This article introduces \pkg{CausalMixGPD}, an \textsf{R} package
that unifies Bayesian nonparametric modeling with EVT, clustering, and
causal analysis in the presence of covariates.  The package provides
three simultaneous capabilities:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[(i)] \textbf{BNP density estimation with an optional GPD
  tail:} the package fits DPMs below a threshold and can splice a
  generalized Pareto tail above it for tail probabilities, extreme
  quantiles, and rare-event prediction.

  \item[(ii)] \textbf{Predictor-dependent clustering:} dedicated
  clustering wrappers support dependence through weights, component
  parameters, or both, and report label-invariant summaries via
  posterior similarity matrices and Dahl representative partitions
  \citep{Binder1978,Dahl2006}.

  \item[(iii)] \textbf{Causal estimation via posterior functionals:}
  treatment-specific outcome distributions are modeled directly, so
  that average, restricted-mean, and quantile treatment effects are
  computed from the fitted distributions in both randomized and
  observational studies, with optional propensity-score augmentation in
  the latter.
\end{itemize}

Taken together, \pkg{CausalMixGPD} offers a unified workflow for
density estimation, prediction, clustering, and causal reporting when
tail behavior is of primary concern.  The package is implemented in
\textsf{R}, with computationally intensive components delegated to
\pkg{nimble} \citep{nimble_pkg}.  The remainder of this article is
organized as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:prelim} introduces the spliced
model and the reported causal estimands.
Section~\ref{sec:overview} describes the package workflow and user
interface.  Sections~\ref{sec:app-clustering}
and~\ref{sec:app-causal} illustrate the clustering and causal
workflows through data analyses, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
concludes with a discussion of limitations and future directions.


\section{Background and Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

Let the outcome--covariate pair be defined by
\begin{equation}
(Y,X) \in \mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R}^p .
\end{equation}
For a fixed covariate value $x$, define
\begin{equation}
F(y\mid x)=\Pr\;(Y\le y\mid X=x),
\qquad
f(y\mid x)=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}F(y\mid x),
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
Q(\tau\mid x)=\inf\Bigl\{y\in\mathbb{R}:\,F(y\mid x)\ge \tau\Bigr\},
\qquad 0<\tau<1.
\end{equation}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} treats $F(\cdot\mid x)$ as the primary inferential
object.  The center of the distribution is modeled nonparametrically,
and the upper tail can optionally be replaced by a generalized Pareto
component when extreme-value extrapolation is required.  All reported
quantities---densities, survival curves, quantiles, means, and causal
contrasts---are posterior functionals of this fitted distribution.



\subsection{Generalized Pareto Distribution for Threshold Exceedances}
\label{subsec:gpd}

The tail region is defined by values exceeding a high threshold.
Extreme value theory provides a standard approximation for such
exceedances.  Let $u(x)$ denote a high threshold, potentially
depending on $x$, and define the exceedance
\begin{equation}
Z = Y - u(x),
\end{equation}
considered conditionally on $Y > u(x)$.  The conditional excess
distribution is
\begin{equation}
F_u(z\mid x) = \Pr\!\bigl(Y-u(x) \le z \mid Y>u(x), X=x\bigr),
\qquad z \ge 0.
\label{eq:cedf}
\end{equation}
Under broad regularity conditions, $F_u(\cdot\mid x)$ is
well-approximated by a generalized Pareto distribution (GPD) when
$u(x)$ is sufficiently large
\citep{BalkemaDeHaan1974,Pickands1975,Coles2001}.

For $y>u(x)$, the corresponding GPD cumulative distribution function is
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{GPD}}\bigl(y\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi\bigr)=
\begin{cases}
1-\left(1+\xi\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right)^{-1/\xi}, & \xi\neq 0,\\[8pt]
1-\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right), & \xi=0,
\end{cases}
\label{eq:gpd_cdf}
\end{equation}
with support $y\ge u(x)$ when $\xi\ge 0$, and $u(x)\le y\le
u(x)-\sigma(x)/\xi$ when $\xi<0$.  The corresponding density function
is
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{GPD}}(y\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{1}{\sigma(x)}\left(1+\xi\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right)^{-1/\xi-1}, & \xi\neq 0,\\[10pt]
\dfrac{1}{\sigma(x)}\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right), & \xi=0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:gpd_pdf}
\end{equation}
The associated GPD quantile function takes the form
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{GPD}}(\tau\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi)=
\begin{cases}
u(x)-\sigma(x)\log(1-\tau), & \xi=0,\\[6pt]
u(x)+\dfrac{\sigma(x)}{\xi}\Bigl((1-\tau)^{-\xi}-1\Bigr), & \xi\neq 0,
\end{cases}
\qquad 0<\tau<1.
\label{eq:gpd_quantile}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Dirichlet Process Mixture Model for Conditional Densities}
\label{subsec:dpm}

Below the threshold $u(x)$, the conditional density of $Y$ given $X$
is modeled using a DPM regression model.  Let
$k(\cdot\mid x;\theta)$ denote a user-chosen kernel family indexed by
parameter~$\theta$.  The hierarchical specification is
\begin{equation}
Y \mid X=x,\theta \sim k(\cdot\mid x;\theta),
\qquad
\theta \mid H \sim H,
\qquad
H \mid \kappa,H_0 \sim \mathrm{DP}(\kappa,H_0),
\label{eq:dpm_hier}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is an unknown mixing distribution on the kernel-parameter
space, $H_0$ is a base measure, and $\kappa>0$ is a concentration
parameter \citep{Ferguson1973}.  Marginalizing over $H$ yields an
(almost surely) discrete mixing distribution together with an infinite
mixture representation of the form
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x)
=
\int k(y\mid x;\theta)\,dH(\theta)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j\,k(y\mid x;\theta_j),
\qquad
w_j\ge 0,
\qquad
\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j=1.
\label{eq:dpm_mixture}
\end{equation}
One representation of the weights is Sethuraman's stick-breaking
construction \citep{Sethuraman1994}, in which
\begin{equation}
w_1=V_1,
\qquad
w_j=V_j\prod_{\ell<j}(1-V_\ell),
\qquad
V_j \stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Beta}(1,\kappa),
\qquad
\theta_j \stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} H_0.
\label{eq:sb}
\end{equation}
An equivalent characterization is obtained by marginalizing over $H$,
yielding the Blackwell--MacQueen P\'olya urn representation.  In
particular, the conditional distribution of a parameter value given all
others can be written as
\begin{equation}
\theta_i \mid \{\theta_j\}_{j \neq i}
\sim
\frac{\kappa}{\kappa+n} H_0(\cdot)
+
\frac{1}{\kappa+n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\delta_{\theta_j}(\cdot),
\label{eq:crp}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{\theta_j}(\cdot)$ denotes a point mass at $\theta_j$.
This specification induces a clustering structure commonly described
via the Chinese restaurant process (CRP) \citep{Neal2000}.  Both the
stick-breaking and CRP representations are widely employed for
posterior simulation in DPM models
\citep{EscobarWest1995,Neal2000}.  Because DPMs operate without
prespecifying the number of mixture components and can capture complex
distributional features such as multimodality, skewness, and
heteroscedasticity through appropriate kernel choices, they constitute
a natural model for the bulk of the conditional distribution where data
are abundant and complex structure may be present.


\subsection{Spliced DPM--GPD Conditional Distribution}
\label{subsec:splice}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} employs a spliced (bulk--tail) construction that
retains the DPM fit below a high threshold and replaces the upper tail
with a GPD component
\citep{Frigessi2002,Behrens2004,Coles2001}.  For a fixed covariate
value~$x$, let $u(x)$ denote the threshold, $\sigma(x)>0$ the tail
scale above that threshold, and $\xi\in\mathbb{R}$ the tail-shape
parameter.  Let $\Theta$ collect the bulk-mixture parameters and
$\Phi$ collect the tail quantities $\{u(x),\sigma(x),\xi\}$.  Write
$F_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta)$ for the bulk conditional CDF and
$F_{\mathrm{GPD}}(y\mid x;\Phi)$ for the exceedance CDF.  Then
\begin{equation}
p_u(x)=F_{\mathrm{DP}}(u(x)\mid x;\Theta)
\label{eq:pu_theta}
\end{equation}
is the fitted bulk mass below the threshold $u(x)$, and the spliced
CDF is
\begin{equation}
F(y\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
F_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta), & y \le u(x),\\[6pt]
p_u(x;\Theta)
+
\bigl\{1-p_u(x;\Theta)\bigr\}\,
F_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y\mid x;\Phi\bigr),
& y>u(x),
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_cdf}
\end{equation}
which is continuous at $u(x)$ by construction.  Differentiating
\eqref{eq:splice_cdf} yields
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta), & y \le u(x),\\[8pt]
\bigl\{1-p_u(x;\Theta)\bigr\}\,
f_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y\mid x;\Phi\bigr),
& y>u(x),
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_pdf}
\end{equation}
where the tail density is rescaled by $1-p_u(x;\Theta)$ to ensure that
$f(\cdot\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)$ integrates to unity.
Figure~\ref{fig:splicedDensityFigure-plot} provides a visual
comparison of a one-component gamma density, a two-component
gamma-mixture density, and a two-component gamma-mixture-plus-GPD
spliced density with the splice point set at the 80th bulk-mixture
quantile.

When the bulk kernel possesses a finite mean and the GPD tail satisfies
$\xi<1$, the ordinary mean of the spliced model admits the closed form
\[
\mathrm{E}(Y\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\int_{-\infty}^{u(x)} y\,f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta)\,dy
\;+\;
\bigl\{1-p_u(x;\Theta)\bigr\}
\left\{u(x)+\frac{\sigma(x)}{1-\xi}\right\}.
\]
This identity underlies the analytical mean calculations employed by
the package whenever the mean exists.

For $\tau \le p_u(x;\Theta)$, quantiles are obtained by inverting the
bulk CDF.  Let $Q_{\mathrm{DP}}(\tau\mid x;\Theta)$ denote the bulk
quantile function.  For $\tau > p_u(x;\Theta)$, the probability index
must be rescaled relative to the exceedance probability, yielding the
rescaled index
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\tau}(x;\Theta)=\dfrac{\tau-p_u(x;\Theta)}{1-p_u(x;\Theta)}\in(0,1),
\label{eq:tau_tilde}
\end{equation}
Combining both cases, the spliced quantile function is
\begin{equation}
Q(\tau\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
Q_{\mathrm{DP}}(\tau\mid x;\Theta), & \tau \le p_u(x;\Theta),\\[8pt]
Q_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(\tilde{\tau}(x;\Theta)\mid x;\Phi\bigr), & \tau>p_u(x;\Theta).
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_quantile_case}
\end{equation}
In subsequent sections, $\Theta$ indexes the bulk DPM and $\Phi$
indexes the tail component; their respective specifications appear in
Sections~\ref{subsec:gpd} and~\ref{subsec:dpm}.


\subsection{Clustering under predictor-dependent Bayesian nonparametric mixtures}
\label{subsec:clustering-theory}

A standard Bayesian nonparametric approach to conditional density
modeling represents the collection
$\{f(\,\cdot\mid x): x\in\mathcal{X}\}$ through predictor-indexed
random probability measures.  Within the Dependent Dirichlet Process
(DDP) framework of \citet{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000}, one
specifies a family $\{G_x: x\in\mathcal{X}\}$ of random mixing
measures whose dependence across~$x$ induces flexible borrowing of
information in $f(\,\cdot\mid x)$.

A generic mixture representation is
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \int k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta\right)\, dG_x(\theta),
\label{eq:clust_mix_int}
\end{equation}
where $k(\cdot\mid x,\theta)$ is a kernel and $G_x$ is a
predictor-indexed mixing measure.  When $G_x$ is discrete (as under
stick-breaking constructions), latent parameters exhibit ties, inducing
a random partition of observations.  Introducing latent cluster-label
variables $z_i$ and component parameters $\{\theta_j\}_{j\ge1}$ such
that
\begin{equation}
y_i \mid z_i,\{\theta_j\},x_i \sim k\!\left(\,\cdot \mid x_i,\theta_{z_i}\right),
\label{eq:clust_latent}
\end{equation}
clustering is characterized by the posterior distribution of the
induced partition $\Pi=\{z_1,\ldots,z_n\}$ and is summarized in a
label-invariant manner through co-clustering functionals
\citep{Binder1978,Dahl2006}.  The clustering component of
\pkg{CausalMixGPD} accommodates three forms of predictor dependence,
each corresponding to a particular construction of $G_x$.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weight dependence only:} Predictors modulate mixture
weights while component parameters remain shared across covariates,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)\, k\!\left(y\mid \theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_weights_only}
\end{equation}
A common implementation uses predictor-dependent stick-breaking, where
stick lengths are transformed regressions in covariates; the logistic
stick-breaking process constructs these lengths via logistic
regressions and maps them to weights \citep{Ren2011}.  Such models can
be viewed as practical DDP instances in which dependence is introduced
through smoothly varying weights
\citep{MacEachern2000,Quintana2022}.

\item \textbf{Parameter dependence only:} Predictors enter through
component-specific kernel parameters while weights remain global,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j\, k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_param_only}
\end{equation}
This structure aligns with DDP constructions that share weights while
permitting predictor-indexed parameters
\citep{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000}, and is closely related to
ANOVA-type dependent priors for structured collections of random
measures \citep{DeIorio2004}.

\item \textbf{Weight and parameter dependence:} The most flexible
specification allows predictors to influence both cluster prevalence
and within-cluster behavior,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)\, k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_both}
\end{equation}
This corresponds to DDP-type models in which both selection
probabilities and component-specific conditional structure vary with
predictors \citep{Quintana2022}.
\end{itemize}

Posterior clustering summaries are reported through label-invariant
functionals.  The posterior similarity matrix (PSM) has entries
\begin{equation}
S_{i\ell} \;=\; \Pr(z_i=z_\ell \mid \text{data}), \qquad i,\ell=1,\ldots,n,
\label{eq:psm}
\end{equation}
estimated by Monte Carlo averages over MCMC output.  A representative
partition is obtained by selecting the sampled partition whose
adjacency matrix is closest to the PSM under squared loss
\citep{Dahl2006}, yielding a stable point estimate while retaining the
PSM as the primary uncertainty summary.
Section~\ref{subsec:cluster_extension} provides further details on the
package's implementation of these summaries.


\subsection{Causal Inference: Notation, Identification, and Reported Estimands}
\label{subsec:causal}

We adopt the potential outcomes framework
\citep{Neyman1923,Rubin1974,ImbensRubin2015}.  Let $A\in\{0,1\}$
denote a binary treatment indicator ($A=1$ treated, $A=0$ control),
let $X$ denote pre-treatment covariates, and let $Y(a)$ denote the
potential outcome under arm $a\in\{0,1\}$.  The observed outcome
satisfies
\begin{equation}
Y = A\,Y(1) + \bigl(1-A\bigr)\,Y(0).
\label{eq:po_observed}
\end{equation}

The treatment-specific conditional distribution functions are the
fundamental inferential objects.  For each arm $a\in\{0,1\}$, the
arm-specific conditional CDF is
\begin{equation}
F_a(y\mid x)=\Pr\;\{Y(a)\le y \mid X=x\},\qquad a\in\{0,1\}.
\label{eq:po_cond_cdf}
\end{equation}
In \pkg{CausalMixGPD}, the corresponding conditional densities
$f_a(y\mid x)$ assume the spliced DPM structure
in~\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}.  The conditional $\tau$-quantile is obtained
by inverting $F_a(\cdot\mid x)$,
\begin{equation}
Q_a(\tau\mid x)=\inf\{y:\,F_a(y\mid x)\ge \tau\},\qquad \tau\in(0,1),
\label{eq:po_cond_quantile}
\end{equation}
and the conditional mean is a functional of the conditional density,
\begin{equation}
\mu_a(x)=\mathrm{E}\{Y(a)\mid X=x\}=\int y\, f_a(y\mid x)\,dy.
\label{eq:po_cond_mean}
\end{equation}

Marginal (standardized) distributions are obtained by averaging
conditional distributions over a covariate distribution.  Writing
$F_a^{\mathrm{m}}$ for the population-standardized arm-$a$ CDF and
$F_a^{\mathrm{t}}$ for the treated-standardized analog, these are
defined by
\begin{equation}
F_a^{\mathrm{m}}(y)=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{F_a(y\mid X)\bigr\},
\qquad
F_a^{\mathrm{t}}(y)=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{F_a(y\mid X)\bigm\vert A=1\bigr\}.
\label{eq:marginal_cdf_defs}
\end{equation}
The associated marginal quantile functions are obtained by inverting
these standardized CDFs,
\begin{equation}
Q_a^{\mathrm{m}}(\tau)=\inf\{y:\,F_a^{\mathrm{m}}(y)\ge \tau\},
\qquad
Q_a^{\mathrm{t}}(\tau)=\inf\{y:\,F_a^{\mathrm{t}}(y)\ge \tau\},
\label{eq:marginal_quantile_defs}
\end{equation}
while the marginal means integrate the conditional mean over the
respective covariate distributions,
\begin{equation}
\mu_a^{\mathrm{m}}=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{\mu_a(X)\bigr\},
\qquad
\mu_a^{\mathrm{t}}=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{\mu_a(X)\mid A=1\bigr\}.
\label{eq:marginal_mean_defs}
\end{equation}

Identification of these quantities depends on the study design.  In
randomized experiments, randomization together with positivity implies
exchangeability between treatment assignment and potential outcomes
\citep{ImbensRubin2015}.  In observational studies, identification
relies on the following conditions
\citep{RosenbaumRubin1983,ImbensRubin2015}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA):} there
is no interference between units and no hidden versions of treatment.
\item \textbf{Consistency:} if $A=a$, then the observed outcome equals
the corresponding potential outcome, that is, $Y=Y(a)$.
\item \textbf{Conditional ignorability:} treatment assignment is
as-if randomized given covariates, so that
\begin{equation}
\bigl(Y(0),Y(1)\bigr)\ \perp\ A \ \mid\ X.
\label{eq:ignorability}
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Overlap (positivity):} every covariate profile has a
strictly positive probability of receiving either treatment,
\begin{equation}
0<\Pr\;(A=1\mid X=x)<1
\label{eq:overlap}
\end{equation}
for all covariate values of interest.
\end{itemize}

Under these conditions, the treatment-specific conditional
distributions are identified from the observed data as
\begin{equation}
F_a(y\mid x)=\Pr\;(Y\le y\mid A=a,X=x),\qquad a\in\{0,1\}.
\label{eq:identification_cdf}
\end{equation}

In observational settings, the package employs the propensity score
(PS) to support the design stage \citep{RosenbaumRubin1983}.  The PS
is the conditional probability of treatment assignment given
covariates,
\begin{equation}
\rho(x)=\Pr\;(A=1\mid X=x),
\label{eq:ps_def}
\end{equation}
and a key property of the true propensity score is that conditioning
on it balances covariates across treatment groups.  Following a
two-stage strategy, the package first estimates $\rho(x)$ using only
$(A,X)$ and then augments the outcome model covariates with the
estimated score, so that the augmented predictor vector is
\begin{equation}
r(x)=\bigl(x^{\mathsf{T}},\widehat{\rho}(x)\bigr)^{\mathsf{T}}.
\label{eq:augmented_covariate}
\end{equation}
The conditional outcome distributions are then modeled separately
within each treatment arm as functions of the augmented predictor
$r(x)$, and causal contrasts are computed as functionals of the
estimated treatment-specific distributions.
Figure~\ref{fig:causalEstimandFigure-plot} illustrates two
treatment-specific conditional densities at a representative covariate
profile.  The dotted verticals mark the 90th conditional quantile for
each arm, and the double-headed arrow indicates the conditional
quantile treatment effect (CQTE) at that level.

\section{Package Overview: Workflow, Model, and Inference}
\label{sec:overview}

This section describes the workflow for fitting conditional models and
obtaining predictions with \pkg{CausalMixGPD}.  The presentation
begins with the one-arm setup, in which the spliced DPM--GPD model is
fitted to an outcome conditional on covariates with a generalized
Pareto distribution governing the upper tail above a
covariate-dependent threshold.  The causal extension augments this
workflow with arm-specific outcome models and an optional propensity
score component, reusing the same estimation and prediction machinery.
Simulated data sets bundled with the package are used throughout to
demonstrate the principal components; these are accessible via
\code{data()}.

\subsection{Spliced DPM--GPD model, likelihood, and priors}
\label{subsec:overview-model}

The observed data consist of $n$ outcome--covariate pairs
$\mathcal{D}=\{(y_i,x_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, where $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^p$
denotes the covariate vector for unit $i$, optionally including an
intercept column.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hlkwd{data}\hldef{(}\hlsng{"nc_posX100_p3_k2"}\hldef{,} \hlkwc{package} \hldef{=} \hlsng{"CausalMixGPD"}\hldef{)}
\hldef{dat} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{data.frame}\hldef{(}\hlkwc{y} \hldef{= nc_posX100_p3_k2}\hlopt{$}\hldef{y, nc_posX100_p3_k2}\hlopt{$}\hldef{X)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

The target conditional distribution $F(\cdot\mid x)$ and its spliced
DPM--GPD construction are defined in Section~\ref{sec:prelim}, with
the spliced CDF and density given in
\eqref{eq:splice_cdf}--\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}.  For posterior
computation, \pkg{CausalMixGPD} approximates the infinite bulk DPM by
a finite truncation to $J$ mixture components, so that
\begin{equation*}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta)\;\approx\;\sum_{j=1}^{J} w_j\,k(y\mid x;\theta_j),
\end{equation*}
where $j=1,\ldots,J$ indexes components, each weight satisfies
$w_j\ge 0$, and the weights sum to one up to a small residual mass
assigned to the remaining components.  Kernel parameters may be
constant, component-specific, or covariate-dependent through link
specifications of the form
\begin{equation}
  g\!\left(\psi_j(x)\right)=x^\top \beta_{j,\psi},
  \label{eq:overview-link}
\end{equation}
where $\psi_j(x)$ denotes a generic kernel parameter for component $j$
at covariate value $x$ and $g(\cdot)$ is a link function that enforces
the parameter support (e.g., a log link for positive scale parameters).
All code examples in this section use the MCMC settings shown below,
which are intentionally abbreviated for illustration; longer chains and
multiple chains are recommended for substantive analyses.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hlkwd{data.frame}\hldef{(mcmc_fixed,}\hlkwc{row.names} \hldef{=} \hlkwa{NULL}\hldef{)}
\end{alltt}
\begin{verbatim}
  niter nburnin thin nchains seed
1  2500     500    1       3 2026
\end{verbatim}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

The one-arm spliced model is fitted by supplying a formula, data, and
control arguments to \code{dpmgpd()}.  The \code{formula} argument
follows standard \textsf{R} syntax and specifies which covariates
enter both the bulk and tail components.  The \code{backend} argument
selects the representation of DPM weights: \code{"sb"} invokes the
stick-breaking representation in~\eqref{eq:sb}, while \code{"crp"}
uses the Chinese restaurant process representation.  The \code{kernel}
argument specifies the bulk kernel family, \code{components}
determines the truncation level, and \code{mcmc} controls the sampler
configuration.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{fit} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{dpmgpd}\hldef{(}\hlkwc{formula} \hldef{= y} \hlopt{~} \hldef{x1} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x2} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x3,} \hlkwc{data} \hldef{= dat,} \hlkwc{mcmc} \hldef{= mcmc_fixed)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

Given the spliced density in~\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}, the likelihood is
\begin{equation}
  L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})
  =\prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i\mid x_i;\Theta,\Phi),
  \label{eq:overview-lik}
\end{equation}
where $\Theta$ collects the bulk parameters
$\{w_j,\theta_j\}_{j=1}^J$ and $\Phi$ collects the tail parameters
governing $u(x)$, $\sigma(x)$, and $\xi$.  The log-likelihood can
equivalently be written as
\begin{align}
  \log L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})
  \;=&\;\sum_{i=1}^n (1-\delta_i)\,\log f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y_i\mid x_i;\Theta)
  \nonumber\\
  &\;+\;\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i
  \Bigl[
    \log\!\bigl\{1-F_{\mathrm{DP}}(u(x_i)\mid x_i;\Theta)\bigr\}
    +\log f_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y_i\mid x_i;\Phi\bigr)
  \Bigr],
  \label{eq:overview-loglik}
\end{align}
where $f_{\mathrm{GPD}}$ is the GPD tail density from
Section~\ref{subsec:splice} and $F_{\mathrm{DP}}(\cdot\mid x)$ is the
bulk mixture CDF.

When the GPD tail is not included, the model reduces to
$f(y\mid x;\Theta, \Phi) \equiv f_{\text{DP}}(y \mid x; \Theta)$, a
mixture over the bulk alone.  A convenience interface to this bulk-only
model is provided by the \code{dpmix()} wrapper.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{fit} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{dpmix}\hldef{(}\hlkwc{formula} \hldef{= y} \hlopt{~} \hldef{x1} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x2} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x3,} \hlkwc{data} \hldef{= dat,} \hlkwc{mcmc} \hldef{= mcmc_fixed)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

Prior distributions for $\Theta$ and $\Phi$ can be specified by the
user; defaults are weakly informative.  For the DPM parameters
$\Theta$, the default prior employs stick-breaking priors for the
mixture weights and conjugate priors for kernel parameters when
conjugacy obtains.  For the tail parameters $\Phi$, the default adopts
a regression-based specification for the threshold $u(x)$ and scale
$\sigma(x)$, together with a weakly informative prior for the shape
parameter~$\xi$.

\subsection{Inference and prediction}
\label{subsec:overview-mcmc}

Posterior simulation targets the joint posterior
$$p(\Theta,\Phi\mid \mathcal{D}) \propto L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})\,p(\Theta,\Phi)$$
using the likelihood~\eqref{eq:overview-lik} and the prior structure
described in Section~\ref{subsec:overview-model}.
\pkg{CausalMixGPD} conducts MCMC via \pkg{nimble}
\citep{nimble_pkg}.  The fitted object supports \code{summary()} for
posterior summaries, \code{plot()} for standard MCMC diagnostics, and
\code{params()} for lightweight parameter extraction.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hlkwd{summary}\hldef{(fit)}  \hlcom{# posterior summaries for parameters and functionals}
\hlkwd{params}\hldef{(fit)}   \hlcom{# extract MCMC samples for parameters and functionals}
\hlkwd{plot}\hldef{(fit,} \hlkwc{family} \hldef{=} \hlsng{"traceplot"}\hldef{,} \hlkwc{params} \hldef{=} \hlsng{"alpha"}\hldef{)} \hlcom{# traceplot for concentration parameter}
\hlkwd{plot}\hldef{(fit,} \hlkwc{family} \hldef{=} \hlsng{"auto"}\hldef{)} \hlcom{# automatic selection of diagnostic plots for all parameters}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

The package provides posterior summaries for several functionals of the
fitted conditional distribution defined in
Section~\ref{sec:prelim}: the density $f(y\mid x)$, survival function
$S(y\mid x) = 1 - F(y\mid x)$, quantile function $Q(\tau\mid x)$,
and mean $\mathrm{E}(y\mid x)$.  For the \code{density},
\code{survival}, and \code{quantile} types, each functional is
evaluated draw-by-draw from
\eqref{eq:splice_cdf}--\eqref{eq:splice_quantile_case}, and the
retained draws are then summarized.  For \code{type = "mean"}, the
package employs analytical draw-level calculations whenever the kernel
mean exists and, for spliced GPD models, the tail-shape parameter
satisfies $\xi < 1$; if these conditions are not met, the ordinary
mean is not reported and the user is directed to
\code{type = "rmean"}.  The restricted-mean target
(\code{type = "rmean"}) provides a separate posterior predictive Monte
Carlo calculation for a user-supplied cutoff and remains well-defined
even when the ordinary mean does not exist.  The following code
illustrates how posterior density, quantile, and mean summaries are
obtained at observed covariate values, together with credible intervals
for quantiles and HPD intervals for means.

partition, cluster sizes, and assignment scores

handles both training labels and new-observation assignment.

\subsection{Causal extension and propensity score augmentation}
\label{subsec:overview-causal}

The causal framework fits separate outcome models for each treatment
arm and can optionally augment the outcome covariates with a
propensity-score summary.  When propensity-score augmentation is
required (as in observational studies), treatment assignment is modeled
as
\begin{equation*}
A_i \mid \boldsymbol{x}_i \sim
\mathrm{Bernoulli}\big(e_{\boldsymbol{\gamma}}(\boldsymbol{x}_i)\big),
\end{equation*}
and the estimated propensity score is appended to the covariate vector
to form the augmented predictor
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{x})
=
\Big(\boldsymbol{x}^\top,\ \psi\big(\widehat e(\boldsymbol{x})\big)\Big)^\top,
\end{equation*}
where the PS link function (logit or probit) is specified through the
\code{PS} argument and the transformation $\psi(\cdot)$ applied to the
estimated score is controlled by \code{ps\_scale}, with the default
being the identity.  The outcome model for each arm is then a spliced
DPM--GPD regression on the augmented covariates, reusing exactly the
same mixture-regression and GPD-tail machinery as the one-arm workflow
described in Section~\ref{subsec:overview-model}.  For randomized
experiments, the PS component is unnecessary and the same modeling
workflow is applied to each arm without augmentation.

The code below loads a simulated benchmark data set and assembles the
data frame expected by the causal wrappers, with columns for the
outcome, treatment indicator, and covariates.



The call to \code{dpmgpd.causal()} fits arm-specific spliced mixture
models using the same structure as \code{dpmgpd()}, with additional
arguments controlling the propensity-score model (\code{PS},
\code{ps\_scale}, \code{ps\_summary}) and separate MCMC settings for
the outcome and PS stages (\code{mcmc\_outcome}, \code{mcmc\_ps}).

\section{Data Analysis~I: Clustering}
\label{sec:app-clustering}

In this section, the procedure is explained for conducting clustering in the context of a conditional mixture model for the case of a bulk-only setting. The goal is to discover clusters amongst the observational units through the use of the fitted mixture model, while simultaneously accounting for uncertainty in the structure of such clusters in a label invariant way. While previously we focused on illustrating how prediction can be conducted through predictive densities in these models, here our interest lies in partition summaries and predicting cluster memberships in out-of-sample data. Important results include the posterior similarity matrix (PSM), a summary of partitions, and cluster memberships of out-of-sample data.

\subsection{Data and preprocessing}

Predictors used include the lstat, rm, and nox variables, which represent the level of neighborhood disadvantage, the size of the houses, and pollution levels, respectively. This combination of predictors represents a sensible choice for discovering representative clusters. We are interested in uncovering representative clusters from amongst the data, and predicting which latent clusters held-out census tracts belong to using out-of-sample prediction. \subsection{Model and fit}

Arm-specific conditional outcome distributions
$F_a(\cdot\mid \boldsymbol{x})$ are modeled via the spliced bulk--tail
construction described in Section~\ref{subsec:splice}:
\begin{equation*}
f_a(y\mid \boldsymbol{x})
=
\sum_j w_{aj}(\boldsymbol{x})\, k(y\mid \Theta_{aj})\,\mathbf{1}(y\le u)
+
\{1-F_a(u\mid \boldsymbol{x})\}\, g(y\mid u,\sigma_a,\xi_a)\,\mathbf{1}(y>u).
\end{equation*}
The bulk kernel $k(\cdot)$ represents a Dirichlet process mixture
regression with a Chinese restaurant process backbone, while the tail
density $g(\cdot)$ follows the GPD distribution with positive scale
$\sigma_a>0$ and tail index $\xi_a$. In our model, the GPD tail index
$\xi_a$ regulates extremal weight (e.g., if $\xi_a>0$, it indicates a
heavier tail). Our model mitigates estimation instabilities for extreme
quantiles through nonparametric bulk and EVT-consistent tail modeling.

Here, a two-sample mixture is fitted with the gamma bulk kernel and the
same MCMC configuration across the analysis. The aim of this analysis is
not to make any final conclusions but to highlight how the package
provides standardized and conditional Lalonde earnings contrasts under
the spliced DPM-GPD framework.

\subsection{Results and interpretation}

Two main questions that have to be answered in the interpretation of the results concern the general effects of the program (the question of interest here) on the earnings distribution, as well as the differences between effects for different profiles of participants. In this regard, the answer to the first question does not provide anything significant. The average treatment effect is close to zero, and the estimated QTE are negative at lower and mid-quantiles. On the other hand, the second question is answered in somewhat mixed manner – treatment effects are profile-specific, and some CQTE at upper quantiles show a positive effect which seems to be true only for some particular subpopulations.

In general, this analysis emphasizes the importance of using distributional analysis in contexts of heavy-tailed data. A simple analysis that concerns only the mean will provide little evidence of a program effect. Meanwhile, QTE summary allows us to observe the heterogeneity of treatment effects.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} integrates flexible bulk density estimation,
optional EVT-based tail extrapolation, predictor-dependent clustering,
and distributional causal inference within a single coherent workflow.
The same fitted model supports posterior summaries for densities,
survival curves, analytical means (when they exist), restricted means,
quantiles, and treatment contrasts, thereby obviating the fragmented
analysis pipelines often required when heavy tails and distributional
effects are both of concern.

The package is considerably broader than the spliced examples
emphasized in this article.  Users may work with bulk-only or spliced
models, multiple kernel families on the real line or on positive
support, stick-breaking or CRP backends, and clustering or causal
wrappers built upon the same predictive machinery.  This shared
infrastructure facilitates transitions from exploratory subgroup
analysis to tail-focused prediction and causal reporting without
altering the underlying modeling language.

Several practical limitations warrant acknowledgment.  First,
computation can be demanding.  Mixture models with covariate
dependence, optional tail components, and arm-specific causal fits are
inherently MCMC-intensive, and costs escalate further with sensitivity
analyses, longer chains, or richer predictor sets.

Second, model tuning requires attention.  A spliced analysis inherits
specification choices from both the DPM and peaks-over-threshold
components, including kernel family, truncation level, threshold
specification, and the number of exceedances available for tail
learning.  When overlap is weak or exceedances are sparse, posterior
uncertainty can be substantial; for clustering, the posterior
similarity matrix is often more informative than any single partition
summary.

Third, the current scope is circumscribed.  The causal implementation
is restricted to binary treatments and continuous outcomes under
standard consistency, ignorability, and overlap assumptions.
Propensity-score augmentation can improve covariate adjustment in
observational studies, but it is not a substitute for careful study
design, overlap diagnostics, or sensitivity analysis for unmeasured
confounding.

\subsection{Model fitting options and prior specifications}
\label{subsec:app-modeling}

Besides the customizable features illustrated in Sections \ref{subsec:gpd}, \ref{subsec:dpm}, \ref{subsec:causal}, and \ref{subsec:overview-model}, this section provides a brief introduction to arguments that allow kernel choice, latent structure, and prior customization.

\subsubsection*{Kernel families and support}
\label{subsec:app-kernels}
The argument \code{kernel} allows to choose among seven different kernel families, which are either defined with positive support or the real line. The kernels with positive support are \code{"gamma"}, \code{"lognormal"}, \code{"invgauss"}, and \code{"amoroso"}. These four kernels provide the respective distribution functions needed for mixture model workflow. The remaining three kernels with the real support are \code{"normal"}, \code{"laplace"}, and \code{"cauchy"}. Because the moments for these three kernels do not exist, they cannot be used for GPD splicing. Function \code{kernel_support_table()} gives information of the support, whether the kernel is compatible with GPD or not, the support of the covariates, and the support of the backend. The function \code{get_kernel_registry()} gives the kernel registry with detailed information such as the parameter names, the default mode of prior, and the density/CDF/quantile functions.

\subsubsection*{Backend type and truncation rule}
\label{subsec:app-backends}
The argument \code{backend} determines the structure of the model for mixing coefficients. The argument \code{sb} stands for the truncated stick-breaking scheme while \code{crp} denotes CRP latent allocation scheme. The latter is a variant of the former with argument \code{GPD = TRUE}. This backend allows for thresholds, tail scales, and tail shapes per mixture component. For causal models, the argument \code{backend} can be either a string of one kind or a vector of length two because different causal arms can have different latent allocation schemes. Arguments \code{components} and \code{epsilon} give the maximum number of mixture components (default to be the size of the sample) and the threshold, respectively. Note that these two arguments can be customized separately for each causal arm.

\subsubsection*{Parameter specification and prior setting}
\label{subsec:app-priors}
The argument \code{param_specs} indicates a named list giving the parameters of the kernel or tail distribution. The element of this argument includes kernel/tail parameter specifications. Kernel/tail parameter has three types of modes, namely, \code{"fixed"}, \code{"dist"}, and \code{"link"}. In the case of using \code{dist}, prior can adopt normal, gamma, inverse-gamma, or log-normal distribution. Alternatively, with mode being \code{"link"}, the parameter of kernel/tail will be related through a linear regression with the covariates based on the five types of link functions, that is, \code{"identity"}, \code{"exp"}, \code{"log"}, \code{"softplus"}, and \code{"power"}. Here the link power will be \code{link_power}. Kernel/tail parameter specification is provided in the default registry. The arguments with the same structure can be used for thresholds, tail scales, and tail shapes, which can take some fixed values where necessary.

\subsubsection{Kernels' families and support}
\label{subsec:app-kernels}

The choice of the kernel (parametric family used in the bulk part of the DP mixture) should come from one of seven families and is specified in \code{kernel} argument. The selected kernel will define the parametric family, which will be utilized to represent each component in the mixture, depending on the support and type of the outcome variable under investigation.

There are three kernels that could be used to fit the outcomes having positive support. The \code{"gamma"} kernel works with the shape-scale parameterization and suits the skewed outcomes (such as income, costs, and durations). \code{"lognormal"} kernel takes the outcome variable in logs, uses the mean-log and std.dev-log parameters. \code{"invgauss"} kernel uses the inverse Gaussian distribution with mean-shape parametrization. There is another four-parametric kernel called \code{"amoroso"}, which fits the right-skewed, positive-supported outcomes and generalizes both \code{"gamma"} and Weibull families. In similar fashion to \code{"gamma"}, \code{"lognormal"}, and \code{"invgauss"}, \code{"amoroso"} kernel allows modeling positive-valued outcomes.

There are three kernels used to model real-valued, possibly unbounded outcomes. The \code{"normal"} kernel uses the normal distribution parameterized by mean and std.dev. The \code{"laplace"} kernel uses double-exponential distribution with location-scale parametrization at component level; it has heavier tails than \code{"normal"}. Finally, \code{"cauchy"} kernel models the data using the Cauchy distribution and location-scale parameters. As the Cauchy distribution has undefined moments and heavy tails, no GPD splicing extension is possible with the \code{"cauchy"} kernel.

Details of the kernels are accessible by calling \code{kernel\_support\_table()}, which returns data.frame with the description of kernels (including the possibility to use GPD extension, component-level covariates availability, and back-end representation). Information about the kernels' characteristics (names of the parameters, support, default prior mode, probability functions) can be accessed through the \code{get\_kernel\_registry()} function.

\subsubsection{Back-end representations}
\label{subsec:app-backends}

\code{backend} argument controls internal representation of the Dirichlet process mixture's weights and component parameters. There are two main back-ends available, with special case for component-level tail parameterization.

The stick-breaking back-end \code{backend = "sb"} implements the truncation of the stick-breaking representation of the Dirichlet process mixture (Section~\ref{subsec:dpm}). The mixture weights are represented explicitly as a truncation of Beta-distributed stick-breaking fractions; \code{components} argument controls the truncation level. This back-end is more suitable when an analyst wants to work with explicit finite collection of mixture components and trace the component weights explicitly.

Chinese restaurant process back-end \code{backend = "crp"} implements the truncation of the CRP based Dirichlet process mixture. In contrast to the stick-breaking back-end, this back-end does not keep track of mixture weights explicitly but allocates the observations to clusters, the number of which is estimated by the data and concentration parameter. The \code{components} argument controls the upper limit for the truncation level for the approximation. It is preferred in case of unknown or potentially large number of sub-populations, since in this back-end there is no need to keep the parameters of unused components.

Another back-end, \code{backend = "spliced"} implements the special case of the Chinese restaurant process back-end with the component-level tail parameterization with the GPD. In this back-end, the threshold, tail scale, and tail shape parameters of the GPD become cluster-specific, thus allowing different tail behavior between clusters. This back-end needs \code{GPD = TRUE} to be set and is suitable in case if the tail behavior differs between sub-populations.

In causal analysis, the back-ends can differ between treatment arms. \code{backend} argument can accept a single string (common for both arms) or a character vector of length two specifying different back-ends for both arms.

\subsubsection{Truncation level and number of components}
\label{subsec:app-components}

Argument \code{components} controls the maximum number of mixture components (in the stick-breaking back-end) or maximum number of clusters (CRP-based back-ends). Should be an integer greater than or equal to 2. Unspecified automatically set by the package to the sample size, which gives a conservative estimate of the required truncation level but increases the computational burden.

The effective number of clusters depends on the data, concentration parameter, and truncation level. The truncation level for prediction and inference purposes can be regulated via \code{epsilon} argument, which is a numeric in [0, 1) and defaulting to 0.025. Components whose posterior cumulative weight falls below \((1-\epsilon)\) or posterior individual weight below \(\epsilon\) are ignored and renormalized. Such approach helps to avoid negligible components contributing numerical errors to density estimation, quantiles, and posterior means.

In causal models, both \code{components} and \code{epsilon} can have either one value shared between the arms or length-two vector of two values (one for each treatment arm).


\subsubsection{Prior specification and the parameter-specification interface}
\label{subsec:app-priors}

Every kernel parameter and every tail parameter can be customized
through the \code{param\_specs} argument, which accepts a named list
with two top-level entries: \code{bulk} (for kernel parameters) and
\code{tail} (for GPD parameters).  Each entry is itself a named list
keyed by parameter name, and each parameter specification is a list
that determines how that parameter enters the model.

Three modes are available for each parameter.  In \emph{fixed mode}
(\code{mode = "fixed"}), the parameter is held constant at a
user-supplied value throughout the MCMC, which is useful for
anchoring parameters that are known from external information or for
conducting sensitivity analyses.  In \emph{distribution mode}
(\code{mode = "dist"}), the parameter receives a prior distribution
and is sampled during MCMC; available prior families include the
normal, gamma, inverse-gamma, and lognormal distributions, whose
hyperparameters are specified as additional list elements.  In
\emph{link mode} (\code{mode = "link"}), the parameter is modeled as
a function of covariates through a regression specification of the
form $g(\psi_j(x)) = x^\top \beta_{j,\psi}$, where the link
function~$g(\cdot)$ is controlled by the \code{link} element.
Available link functions include \code{"identity"} (no
transformation), \code{"exp"} (log link, enforcing positivity),
\code{"log"} (exponential link), \code{"softplus"} (a smooth
approximation to the positive part), and \code{"power"} (a
power-function link whose exponent is specified via
\code{link\_power}).  Link mode requires that covariates be present
in the model.

The default prior mode for each parameter is determined by the kernel
registry.  For instance, the normal kernel uses link mode with an
identity link for the component mean (so that the mean is a linear
function of covariates) and distribution mode for the standard
deviation.  The gamma kernel uses distribution mode for the shape
parameter and link mode with an exponential link for the scale
parameter (ensuring positivity through a log-linear predictor).
These defaults are designed to be weakly informative and can be
inspected through \code{get\_kernel\_registry()}.

The tail parameters---threshold, tail scale, and tail
shape---follow the same three-mode interface.  The threshold and
tail scale default to link mode with a log link when covariates are
present, allowing the splice point and tail dispersion to vary
smoothly with the predictor values.  The tail-shape parameter, which
governs the heaviness of the extreme tail, typically uses
distribution mode with a weakly informative prior centered near zero.
Users who wish to fix the threshold at a known quantile of the
data (a common practice in peaks-over-threshold analysis) can do so
by specifying \code{threshold = list(mode = "fixed", value = u0)},
where \code{u0} is the desired threshold value.


\subsubsection{Concentration parameter}
\label{subsec:app-alpha}

The concentration parameter $\kappa$ of the Dirichlet Process is known as \code{alpha} in the output of the package. The concentration parameter $\kappa$ controls the prior expectation about the number of clusters. By default, the concentration parameter is assumed to be a random variable following a Gamma distribution and estimated within the Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm (\code{alpha\_random = TRUE}). By setting \code{alpha\_random = FALSE}, $\kappa$ remains fixed as its starting value. This may be useful for conducting sensitivity analyses or incorporating any knowledge of the number of clusters from outside sources.

enables parallel fitting of the treated and control outcome models.

stored in the fitted object and can be accessed for benchmarking.

For CRP-based backends, the \code{z\_update\_every} argument
controls the frequency (in iterations) at which latent cluster
labels are globally updated.  This can be tuned to balance mixing
quality against computational cost.


\subsubsection{Monitoring and posterior output}
\label{subsec:app-monitors}

The monitor argument specifies the set of parameters that will be included in the posterior sample outputted from the model. There are two pre-defined monitor profiles. The core monitor profile includes only the essential parameters needed for the inference process: the concentration parameter, the cluster weights/labels (if any), and the vectors of kernel and tail parameters. The full monitor profile includes all parameters from the model specification.

Moreover, there are two optional arguments monitor\_latent and monitor\_v. Setting monitor\_latent = TRUE saves the latent variables representing cluster allocations z_1,...,z_n, which are required for clustering analysis of the models that were estimated using other interfaces. In particular, setting the CRP or spliced backend defaults to monitor\_latent = TRUE. Lastly, monitor\_v = TRUE saves also the vector of raw stick-breaking proportions V_1,...,V_{J-1}, where V_j = \exp(u_j)/(1+\sum_{k=1}^{j-1}\exp(u_k)), in addition to the posteriors of the model parameters. This feature is available only for the stick-breaking backend.

\subsubsection{Model fitting interfaces}
\label{subsec:app-interfaces}

Two interfaces are provided for describing the model structure. The formula interface employs standard R formula notation in addition to the data argument:

\begin{verbatim}
fit <- dpmgpd(formula = y ~ x1 + x2, data = dat, kernel = "gamma")
\end{verbatim}

\noindent In this way, the formula is used by default to specify the response variable and the design matrix for the predictors in the model. Note that the model.matrix() function is used in the background to expand interaction terms in formulas, and to create the predictors' design matrix including factor variables.

The matrix interface accepts the vector of responses and the design matrix:

\begin{verbatim}
fit <- dpmgpd(y = y_vec, X = X_mat, kernel = "gamma")
\end{verbatim}

\noindent The advantage of the matrix interface is its capability to use the pre-specified design matrix (e.g., scaled or centered predictors). For causal models, the vector of treatment indicators is defined using the treat argument, which can either be a column name in the data frame (formula interface) or a vector itself (matrix interface).

Last but not least, a three-step strategy can be employed that involves (i) using bundle() function to prepare the model specification and compile the Nimble code, (ii) examining the model specification and/or Nimble code and (iii) running the model by calling the mcmc() method.


\subsubsection{Types of predictions and their associated functionals}
\label{subsec:app-predict}

There are eight types of predictions supported by the predict() method for fitted model objects. Each of them corresponds to a particular functional applied to the posterior predictive density.

- type = "density": This argument returns the posterior predictive density function value f(y | x) on the grid of outcome values specified by the argument y. For every combination of covariate profile and grid points, the posterior density will be calculated using draws from the spliced model and summarized using the mean (with optional credible or HPD intervals). Type is convenient for depicting the conditional distribution of the fitted model at selected covariates values.

- type = "survival": This argument computes the posterior predictive survival function S(y | x) = 1 - F(y | x) at the grid of outcome values. It can be used for the analysis of time-to-event outcomes and exceedance probabilities. The algorithm behind this type of predictions is the same as for the type = "density".

- type = "quantile": This argument computes the inverse cumulative function of the posterior predictive distribution at the grid of probability levels (given by the argument index, equivalently p). The result is the vector of conditional quantiles $Q(\tau | x)$. This requires computing the inverse posterior CDF on draws from the spliced model and ensures that the GPD part of the model defines the right tail of the distribution, while the left tail and middle are defined by the bulk kernel. This type of predictions is especially useful for assessing the entire distribution shape, since estimation of quantiles independently would cause quantile crossing issues.

- type = "mean": This argument returns the posterior predictive mean E(Y | x). If there exists a closed form expression for the mean of the bulk kernel and $\xi < 1$ for the GPD tail shape parameter, the posterior predictive means are calculated exactly using the draws from the spliced model. A warning message is returned and the user is suggested using the restricted mean instead if the mean does not exist or equals $\infty$ (this is when $\xi \geq 1$).

- type = "rmean": This argument produces the posterior predictive restricted mean $E[min(Y, c) | x]$, where the truncation constant $c$ is defined by the argument cutoff. The truncated mean always exists and can be used instead of the standard mean in cases where the latter does not exist or is infinite. This type makes use of posterior predictive MC sampling; the number of the draws is controlled by the argument nsim_mean.

- type = "median": This argument produces the posterior predictive median. It is identical to using type = "quantile" with index = 0.5.

- type = "sample": This argument samples values from the posterior predictive distribution $p(y*|x*, D)$. The number of the simulated observations for each covariate profile is controlled by the argument nsim. The difference between this and other types of predictions is that this one produces the raw simulated values without any summarization.

- type = "fit": This argument produces individual posterior predictive draws per observation (one draw per observation per retained iteration).

\subsubsection{Intervals estimation}
\label{subsec:app-intervals}

Confidence and credible interval estimation is available for all prediction and causal estimation methods using the argument interval. When set to "credible" equal-tailed credible intervals at the selected posterior probability level (given in the argument level, default 0.95) are produced. These intervals are based on empirical quantiles of the posterior sample. When the argument interval is set to "hpd", the shortest possible intervals containing the specified posterior probability are constructed. It can be more convenient for highly skewed posterior distributions. When interval = NULL, no interval construction is performed and only point estimates are returned. The argument probs controls the quantiles to be used in the interval construction (default: c(0.025, 0.5, 0.975)).

Label: subsec:app-causal-estimands

Apart from ate(), qte(), cate(), and cqte(), the package offers several additional causal estimand functions.

The att() function estimates the average treatment effect on the treated by marginalizing over the arm-specific conditional means with respect to the covariate distribution of the treated units alone (as opposed to the whole population). The signature of att() is the same as of ate().

Furthermore, when estimating contrasts based on the restricted mean, the cutoff must be specified. Subsubsection: Propensity score customization
Label: subsec:app-ps

In the context of causal inference on observational data, the causal fittings allow for customization of the PS model with regard to three arguments that control aspects of the PS model itself, its summary, and its inclusion into the outcome model.

The PS argument controls what kind of PS model is fit. PS = "logit" (the default) corresponds to fitting a Bayesian logistic regression of the treatment variable on the covariates. The possible alternative values are: PS = "probit" (Bayesian probit), PS = "naive" (naive Bayes Gaussian classifier), PS = FALSE (for experimental data with guaranteed covariate balance).

The ps_scale argument refers to the scale on which the PS model is augmented to the covariate vector in the outcome model. The available choices are: "logit" (log-odds); "prob" (PS-probability scaled).

The ps_summary argument controls how the posterior distribution of the PS model estimates is summarized into a single number to be used in the outcome model. The available options are: "mean" (the default); "median".

The ps_clamp argument improves numerical stability by clamping any extreme PS values to (ε, 1 − ε) where ε > 0 is a user-defined tolerance specified by the ps_clamp parameter (default: 10^−6). It is useful to clamp the PS values to ensure numerical stability when including the PS model log-odds in the outcome model.

The ps_prior argument specifies the PS regression prior as a normal with mean and SD determined by the ps_prior parameter (default: mean=0, sd=2). By default, it specifies a weakly informative prior on the regression coefficients.

The PS and the outcome models are estimated separately and in different MCMC stages, which means that two MCMC configurations are also needed. These configurations are controlled by the mcmc_ps and mcmc_outcome arguments.


\subsubsection{Clustering workflow and prediction types}
\label{subsec:app-clustering-details}

The clustering functions \code{dpmix.cluster()} and
\code{dpmgpd.cluster()} take an optional \code{type} argument that can
take one of three values, depending on the type of covariate
dependence desired in the model.

Setting \code{type = "weights"} implements covariate dependence in
only the mixture weights through a predictor-dependent stick-breaking
construction, leaving the within-cluster response model invariant. In
this case, the kernel-specific parameters for any given cluster are
identical irrespective of the value of predictors, and only the
prevalence of the cluster changes with the predictors.

Setting \code{type = "param"} implies covariate dependence through
only the kernel-specific parameters, while retaining global mixture
weights.  The within-cluster response distribution now changes based
on predictors through the specification of link functions discussed in
Section~\ref{subsec:app-priors}.

Setting \code{type = "both"} allows both types of predictor dependence
to occur; this is the most flexible setup, but also the most
parameter-rich one.

Predictions in clustering can be computed either via the posterior
similarity matrix or via labeling. To compute a PSM,
\code{type="psm"}, the method computes the posterior probability that
observation $i$ belongs to the same cluster as observation $\ell$,
averaging over all retained draws.  To compute labels,
\code{type="label"} uses the Dahl criterion for assigning labels,
using squared Frobenius distance between the matrix formed from the
PSM and the indicator matrix for the partitions of samples into
clusters.  Labels for new observations are computed by aggregating the
co-clustering posterior predictive probabilities with respect to the
partitions that yield the representative PSM, as determined by the
training data.

The \code{summary()} method for the fitted cluster labels outputs
descriptive summaries of the clusters found, including their sizes and
their covariate/response profiles.

Subsubsection: Plotting Functions
\label{subsec:app-plots}

A variety of customized plotting functions, each for the respective classes of fitted and predicted objects, is available through the ggplot2 package.

For one-arm models, plot() creates MCMC diagnostics depending on the chosen family argument. Acceptable diagnostic families are: "traceplot" (MCMC trace plots); "density" and "histogram" (posterior density estimates or histograms); "running" (running mean plots for testing for stationarity); "autocorrelation" (autocorrelation function plots with a customizable number of lags through nLags); "crosscorrelation" (matrix of cross-correlation between variables); "Rhat" and "grb" (Gelman-Rubin diagnostics of MCMC convergence and Gelman-Brooks-Rubin diagnostic plots, requiring at least two chains); "effective" (effective sample size relative to chain length); "geweke" (tests of stationarity with the Geweke criterion); "caterpillar" (forest plots of posterior intervals); and "pairs" (pairwise MCMC diagnostics). For family="auto", plot() automatically creates a combined plot from the above diagnostics depending on the number of chains. The subset of parameters to be plotted can be controlled with params, using full, abbreviated names or regular expressions.

For causal models, plot() forwards its request to the corresponding one-arm model diagnostic plots, extended with the new arm argument. arm = 1 and arm = "treated" are equivalent to arm = 0 and arm = "control", respectively; while arm = "both" performs the diagnosis for all arms simultaneously.

For estimands obtained with ate(), att() and cate() functions, plot() provides treatment-effect estimation plots with the following three types: type = "effect", type = "arms", and type = "both".

For objects created by qte(), qtt() and cqte(), plot() again provides treatment-effect estimation plots, which come with the same three types: type = "effect", type = "arms", and type = "both". For the QTE case, the type="effect" shows the effect as a function of the quantile level along with uncertainty bounds. On the other hand, for the CATE estimator, the type="arms" option shows two effect lines - for the treatment and control - conditional on covariates. Also, for the CATE effect, the additional facet_by argument exists; the possible values are "tau" and "id" that perform faceting over the quantile level and over covariate profile, respectively.

In the case of cluster models, there are several custom plotting techniques. The PSM object offers heatmap plots via plot() method, where the psm_max_n argument controls the size of matrix that cannot be plotted due to computational constraints. The labels cluster object provides plots in three types: type = "sizes" (bar chart of cluster sizes), type = "certainty" (histogram of assignment certainty, that is, maximal probability of assignment per row/observation), and type = "summary" (combines bar chart with responses and covariates of each cluster).

All plotting functions return ggplot2-based objects for subsequent manipulation with ggplot2 code. Setting global option options(CausalMixGPD.plotly = TRUE) or passing plotly=TRUE to the function turns these plots into interactive plotly objects.

The concrete illustrations shown in Figures \ref{fig:app_cluster_overlay}, \ref{fig:app_cluster_test_sizes}, \ref{fig:qte_plot}, and \ref{fig:cate_plot} showcase exemplary plotting output of the described visualization interface for clusters summary plots, predicted label sizes, QTE summaries, and profile-specific CATE/CQTE contrasts, respectively.

\subsubsection{Model inspection and diagnostics}
\label{subsec:app-summaries}

There are several methods to help inspect the fit and extract posterior
quantities.

For one-arm fits, the \code{summary()} method returns an object that
contains the posterior mean, standard deviation, and user-defined
quantiles (as specified by the \code{probs} argument, with defaults
\code{c(0.025, 0.5, 0.975)}) for all monitored parameters, along
with the estimated effective sample size (ESS).  The optional
\code{pars} argument allows specifying only a subset of the
parameters, matching a regular expression pattern. For causal fits,
the method produces summaries for the propensity-score model (where
specified) and each treatment arm's outcome model separately,
allowing the specification of parameters via the
\code{ps\_pars} argument.

The \code{params()} function returns the posterior means in an
appropriate natural parameterization: single-valued parameters
remain single-valued, components' parameters are collected in a
vector and indexed by component label, and regression coefficients'
parameters are organized as matrices labeled by covariate names. For
causal fits, the result is a list containing elements pertaining to
the treated arm, control arm, and (where appropriate) propensity-score
block.

The \code{fitted()} method returns the posterior predictive mean for
each train observation, and \code{residuals()} computes the residual,
i.e., the difference between the observed outcome and the fitted
value.  These functions are helpful in producing diagnostics for a
visual inspection.

The \code{ess\_summary()} function computes a thorough ESS
analysis, including per-chain ESS (controlled by \code{per\_chain}),
normalizing ESS rate by time spent (by specifying \code{wall\_time}),
and accommodating potentially incomplete parameter vector (\code{robust
= TRUE}).

\subsubsection{Computational considerations}
\label{subsec:app-computation}

Some important options for managing computational efficiency in

\code{parallel\_chains} flag, together with the \code{workers}

full posterior inference procedure.  Finally, the

information.  Additionally, verbose \pkg{nimble} compilation output
can be silenced by setting \code{quiet} flag to \code{TRUE}.

argument set to \code{TRUE}.
