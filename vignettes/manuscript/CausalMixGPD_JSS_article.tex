\title{CausalMixGPD: Bayesian Nonparametric Modeling with Optional Heavy-Tail Augmentation and Causal Extensions}
\Plainauthor{Arnab Aich}
\Plaintitle{CausalMixGPD: Bayesian Conditional Dirichlet Process Mixtures with Generalized Pareto Tails and Causal Extensions}
\Shorttitle{CausalMixGPD}

\Abstract{
Heavy-tailed outcomes arise in settings where rare extreme events drive
risk, cost, or policy decisions. Parametric models often describe the
center of the distribution adequately but can miss extreme behavior,
whereas fully nonparametric models lack principled tail extrapolation.
We develop \pkg{CausalMixGPD}, an \textsf{R} package for Bayesian
modeling of heavy-tailed outcomes that combines Dirichlet process
mixtures for the bulk with optional generalized Pareto tails in a
unified framework. The package supports unconditional and
covariate-dependent mixture models, posterior computation through
Markov chain Monte Carlo in \pkg{nimble}, and arm-specific outcome
models for causal inference. The package reports posterior predictive
summaries for densities, quantiles, means, survival curves, and causal
contrasts, with particular emphasis on extreme quantiles and other
tail-sensitive functionals. The same framework also supports
predictor-dependent clustering under multiple kernel specifications.
In practice, \pkg{CausalMixGPD} provides a practical and extensible
workflow for heavy-tailed density estimation, clustering, and causal
reporting.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Causal inference is often summarized through average effects, but many
applications require distributional comparisons between treatment
regimes. In clinical trials, investigators may want to understand how a
treatment changes outcomes across the outcome scale, including effects
among individuals in the upper tail where events are rare but
consequences can be substantial. This shift from mean-based to
distribution-based causal questions makes causal analysis tightly linked
to how well we can estimate (and predict) outcome distributions under
competing treatment assignments.

A growing \textsf{R} ecosystem supports Bayesian causal inference
through flexible outcome modeling and posterior uncertainty
quantification, including tree-based and model-averaging approaches such
as \href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=bcf}{\textsf{bcf}},
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=bartCause}{\textsf{bartCause}},
and \href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=BCEE}{\textsf{BCEE}}
\citep{bcf_pkg,bartCause_pkg,BCEE_pkg}.
These tools are effective for mean and heterogeneous effect estimation,
but distributional reporting is more demanding: quantiles estimated
separately can cross, and tail-focused contrasts require the full
outcome distribution rather than a collection of marginal summaries.
\pkg{CausalMixGPD} addresses this by modeling outcome densities directly
and deriving quantiles, means, survival curves, and tail summaries from
one posterior predictive object.

Dirichlet Process Mixtures (DPM) provide a versatile nonparametric
solution for density estimation because they can capture multimodality
and latent heterogeneity without fixing the number of mixing components
in advance \citep{EscobarWest1995}. In the \textsf{R} ecosystem,
Bayesian nonparametric mixture modeling is well supported by software
based on Dirichlet process (DP) ideas.
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=dirichletprocess}{\textsf{dirichletprocess}}
provides a flexible interface for specifying DPMs and fitting them by
posterior simulation, yielding posterior inference for mixture weights,
component labels, and predictive densities under user-chosen kernels
\citep{dirichletprocess_pkg}. The package
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=DPpackage}{\textsf{DPpackage}}
offers a broad suite of Bayesian semi- and nonparametric models in
\textsf{R}, including DP-based formulations for marginal and conditional
densities, regression functions, and model-based predictive
distributions \citep{jara2011DPpackage}. More recently,
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=BNPmix}{\textsf{BNPmix}}
emphasizes practical Bayesian nonparametric mixture approaches,
providing posterior inference for clustering structure and
density/regression estimation under DP and Pitman--Yor-type mixture
specifications \citep{canale2021BNPmix}.

That clustering perspective is also central to the present package.
Because DP mixtures induce random partitions through latent allocation
variables, they naturally support model-based subgroup discovery in
addition to density estimation. Bayesian cluster analysis is typically
reported through label-invariant summaries such as pairwise
co-clustering probabilities \citep{Binder1978} and representative
partitions selected by Dahl's least-squares criterion \citep{Dahl2006}.
In regression settings, clustering itself can depend on predictors:
dependent Dirichlet process constructions allow covariates to affect
mixture weights, component-specific parameters, or both
\citep{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000,DeIorio2004,Quintana2022}, and
predictor-dependent stick-breaking schemes provide a practical route for
covariate-modulated cluster prevalence \citep{Ren2011}. Existing
software supports parts of this workflow, but typically focuses on bulk
density estimation and clustering under a single kernel family without
an explicit adjustment for heavy-tailed data. As a result, applications
that require both latent subgroup discovery and tail extrapolation still
need additional modeling outside the standard Bayesian nonparametric
mixture workflow.

Estimating values far in the tail requires special care when data become
scarce, and fitting a single model to heavy-tailed data can distort the
fit in the central region. Extreme value theory (EVT) provides a
principled framework for modeling and extrapolation of tail behavior.
EVT characterizes the asymptotic distribution of
threshold exceedances, which can be used to estimate tail probabilities
and quantiles beyond the range of observed data
\citep{BalkemaDeHaan1974,Pickands1975,Coles2001}. Bayesian
implementations of EVT are available in \textsf{R} when posterior
uncertainty quantification for tail quantities is required.
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=evdbayes}{\textsf{evdbayes}}
provides Bayesian inference for standard extreme-value models via
posterior simulation, enabling posterior and posterior-predictive
summaries for extreme-event quantities \citep{evdbayes_pkg}. For
peaks-over-threshold (POT) approaches, the package
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=POT}{\textsf{POT}} implements
a focused collection of tools for GPD modeling and threshold-based tail
analysis \citep{POT_pkg}. For extreme quantile estimation and rare-event
tail summaries,
\href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=extremefit}{\textsf{extremefit}}
provides a software implementation targeted at extreme-quantile
estimation \citep{durrieu2018extremefit}. Existing tools typically
emphasize either flexible bulk modeling or dedicated tail analysis.
Applications that need both predictor-dependent clustering and causal
contrasts in the upper tail still require users to stitch together
multiple workflows.

This article introduces an \textsf{R} package (\pkg{CausalMixGPD}) that
connects Bayesian nonparametric modeling with EVT, clustering,
and robust causal analyses in the presence of covariates (confounders).
The package provides three simultaneous capabilities:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[(i)] \textbf{BNP density estimation with an optional GPD tail:}
  the package fits DPMs below a threshold and can splice a generalized
  Pareto tail above it for tail probabilities, extreme quantiles, and
  rare-event prediction.
  \item[(ii)] \textbf{Predictor-dependent clustering:} dedicated
  clustering wrappers support dependence through weights, component
  parameters, or both, and report label-invariant summaries through
  posterior similarity matrices and Dahl representative partitions
  \citep{Binder1978,Dahl2006}.
  \item[(iii)] \textbf{Causal estimation via posterior functionals:}
  treatment-specific outcome distributions are modeled directly, so
  average, restricted-mean, and quantile treatment effects are computed
  from the fitted distributions in randomized and observational studies,
  with optional propensity-score augmentation in the latter.
 \end{itemize}

Overall, \pkg{CausalMixGPD} offers a unified workflow for
density estimation, prediction, clustering, and causal reporting when
tail behavior matters. The package is implemented in \textsf{R}, with
computationally intensive components integrated through
\pkg{nimble} \citep{nimble_pkg}. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} introduces
the spliced model and the reported causal estimands.
Section~\ref{sec:overview} summarizes the package workflow and user
interface. Sections~\ref{sec:app-clustering} and~\ref{sec:app-causal}
illustrate the clustering and causal workflows, and
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} closes with limitations and future
directions.


\section{Background and Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

Let the outcome--covariate pair be defined by
\begin{equation}
(Y,X) \in \mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R}^p .
\end{equation}
For a fixed covariate value $x$, define
\begin{equation}
F(y\mid x)=\Pr\;(Y\le y\mid X=x),
\qquad
f(y\mid x)=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}F(y\mid x),
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
Q(\tau\mid x)=\inf\Bigl\{y\in\mathbb{R}:\,F(y\mid x)\ge \tau\Bigr\},
\qquad 0<\tau<1.
\end{equation}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} treats $F(\cdot\mid x)$ as the primary inferential
object. The center of the distribution is modeled nonparametrically, and
the upper tail can be replaced by a generalized Pareto component when
extreme-value extrapolation is needed. Reported quantities such as
densities, survival curves, quantiles, means, and causal contrasts are
posterior functionals of this fitted distribution.




\subsection{Generalized Pareto Distribution for Threshold Exceedances}
\label{subsec:gpd}

In modeling terms, the tail region is defined by values that exceed a
high threshold. Extreme value theory gives a standard approximation for
exceedances over such a threshold. Let $u(x)$ be a high threshold,
which may depend on $x$, and define the exceedance
\begin{equation}
Z = Y - u(x),
\end{equation}
considered conditionally on $Y > u(x)$. The conditional excess
distribution is given by
\begin{equation}
F_u(z\mid x) = \Pr\!\bigl(Y-u(x) \le z \mid Y>u(x), X=x\bigr),
\qquad z \ge 0.
\label{eq:cedf}
\end{equation}
Under broad regularity conditions, $F_u(\cdot\mid x)$ is
well-approximated by a generalized Pareto distribution (GPD) when
$u(x)$ is large \citep{BalkemaDeHaan1974,Pickands1975,Coles2001}.

For $y>u(x)$, the corresponding GPD cumulative distribution function is
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{GPD}}\bigl(y\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi\bigr)=
\begin{cases}
1-\left(1+\xi\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right)^{-1/\xi}, & \xi\neq 0,\\[8pt]
1-\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right), & \xi=0,
\end{cases}
\label{eq:gpd_cdf}
\end{equation}
with support $y\ge u(x)$ when $\xi\ge 0$, and $u(x)\le y\le
u(x)-\sigma(x)/\xi$ when $\xi<0$. The corresponding density function is
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{GPD}}(y\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{1}{\sigma(x)}\left(1+\xi\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right)^{-1/\xi-1}, & \xi\neq 0,\\[10pt]
\dfrac{1}{\sigma(x)}\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{y-u(x)}{\sigma(x)}\right), & \xi=0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:gpd_pdf}
\end{equation}
The associated GPD quantile function takes the form
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{GPD}}(\tau\mid u(x),\sigma(x),\xi)=
\begin{cases}
u(x)-\sigma(x)\log(1-\tau), & \xi=0,\\[6pt]
u(x)+\dfrac{\sigma(x)}{\xi}\Bigl((1-\tau)^{-\xi}-1\Bigr), & \xi\neq 0,
\end{cases}
\qquad 0<\tau<1.
\label{eq:gpd_quantile}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Dirichlet Process Mixture Model for Conditional Densities}
\label{subsec:dpm}

In this package, the conditional density of $Y$ given $X$ below a
specific threshold $u(x)$ is modeled using a DPM regression model. Let
$k(\cdot\mid x;\theta)$ be a user-chosen kernel family indexed by
parameter $\theta$. The hierarchical specification is
\begin{equation}
Y \mid X=x,\theta \sim k(\cdot\mid x;\theta),
\qquad
\theta \mid H \sim H,
\qquad
H \mid \kappa,H_0 \sim \mathrm{DP}(\kappa,H_0),
\label{eq:dpm_hier}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is an unknown mixing distribution on the kernel-parameter
space, $H_0$ is a base measure, and $\kappa>0$ is a concentration
parameter \citep{Ferguson1973}. Marginalizing $H$ yields an
(almost surely) discrete mixing distribution together with an infinite
mixture representation of the form
\begin{equation}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x)
=
\int k(y\mid x;\theta)\,dH(\theta)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j\,k(y\mid x;\theta_j),
\qquad
w_j\ge 0,
\qquad
\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j=1.
\label{eq:dpm_mixture}
\end{equation}
One representation for the weights is Sethuraman's stick-breaking
construction \citep{Sethuraman1994}, in which
\begin{equation}
w_1=V_1,
\qquad
w_j=V_j\prod_{\ell<j}(1-V_\ell),
\qquad
V_j \stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Beta}(1,\kappa),
\qquad
\theta_j \stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} H_0.
\label{eq:sb}
\end{equation}
An equivalent characterization is obtained by marginalizing over $H$,
yielding the Blackwell--MacQueen P\'olya urn representation. In
particular, the conditional distribution of a parameter value given the
others can be written as
\begin{equation}
\theta_i \mid \{\theta_j\}_{j \neq i}
\sim
\frac{\kappa}{\kappa+n} H_0(\cdot)
+
\frac{1}{\kappa+n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\delta_{\theta_j}(\cdot),
\label{eq:crp}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{\theta_j}(\cdot)$ is a point mass assigned to $\theta_j$.
This induces a clustering structure often described via the Chinese
restaurant process (CRP) \citep{Neal2000}. Both
stick-breaking and CRP representations are widely used for posterior
simulation in DPM models \citep{EscobarWest1995,Neal2000}. DPMs work
without specifying the number of mixture components in advance and can
capture complex distributional features such as multimodality, skewness,
and heteroscedasticity by choosing an appropriate kernel family, making
them a natural choice for modeling the bulk of the conditional
distribution where data are plentiful and complex features may be
present.


\subsection{Spliced DPM--GPD Conditional Distribution}
\label{subsec:splice}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} uses a spliced (bulk--tail) construction that retains
the DPM fit below a high threshold and replaces the upper tail by a GPD
component \citep{Frigessi2002,Behrens2004,Coles2001}. For a fixed
covariate value $x$, let $u(x)$ denote the threshold, let
$\sigma(x)>0$ denote the tail scale above that threshold, and let
$\xi\in\mathbb{R}$ denote the tail-shape parameter. Let $\Theta$
collect the bulk-mixture parameters and let $\Phi$ collect the tail
quantities $\{u(x),\sigma(x),\xi\}$. Write
$F_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta)$ for the bulk conditional CDF and
$F_{\mathrm{GPD}}(y\mid x;\Phi)$ for the exceedance CDF. Then
\begin{equation}
p_u(x)=F_{\mathrm{DP}}(u(x)\mid x;\Theta)
\label{eq:pu_theta}
\end{equation}
is the fitted bulk mass below the threshold $u(x)$, and the spliced CDF
is
\begin{equation}
F(y\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
F_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta), & y \le u(x),\\[6pt]
p_u(x;\Theta)
+
\bigl\{1-p_u(x;\Theta)\bigr\}\,
F_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y\mid x;\Phi\bigr),
& y>u(x),
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_cdf}
\end{equation}
This construction is continuous at $u(x)$. Differentiating
\eqref{eq:splice_cdf} gives
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta), & y \le u(x),\\[8pt]
\bigl\{1-p_u(x;\Theta)\bigr\}\,
f_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y\mid x;\Phi\bigr),
& y>u(x),
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_pdf}
\end{equation}
where the tail density is rescaled by $1-p_u(x;\Theta)$ so that
$f(\cdot\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)$ integrates to one. Figure
\ref{fig:splicedDensityFigure-plot} gives a compact visual comparison
between a one-component gamma density, a two-component gamma-mixture
density, and a two-component gamma-mixture-plus-GPD spliced density
with the splice set at the 80\% bulk quantile.

For $\tau \le p_u(x;\Theta)$, quantiles are obtained by inverting the
bulk CDF. Let $Q_{\mathrm{DP}}(\tau\mid x;\Theta)$ denote that bulk
quantile function. For $\tau > p_u(x;\Theta)$, the probability index
must be rescaled relative to the exceedance probability, giving the
rescaled index
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\tau}(x;\Theta)=\dfrac{\tau-p_u(x;\Theta)}{1-p_u(x;\Theta)}\in(0,1),
\label{eq:tau_tilde}
\end{equation}
The GPD quantile is
\begin{equation*}
Q_{\mathrm{GPD}}(\tau\mid x;\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
u(x)-\sigma(x)\log(1-\tau), & \xi=0,\\
u(x)+\dfrac{\sigma(x)}{\xi}\bigl\{(1-\tau)^{-\xi}-1\bigr\}, & \xi\neq 0,
\end{cases}
\qquad 0<\tau<1.
\end{equation*}
Combining both cases, the spliced quantile function is
\begin{equation}
Q(\tau\mid x;\Theta,\Phi)=
\begin{cases}
Q_{\mathrm{DP}}(\tau\mid x;\Theta), & \tau \le p_u(x;\Theta),\\[8pt]
Q_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(\tilde{\tau}(x;\Theta)\mid x;\Phi\bigr), & \tau>p_u(x;\Theta).
\end{cases}
\label{eq:splice_quantile_case}
\end{equation}
In later sections, $\Theta$ indexes the bulk DPM and $\Phi$ indexes the
tail component. The GPD and DPM specifications are introduced in
Sections~\ref{subsec:gpd} and~\ref{subsec:dpm} above.


\subsection{Clustering under predictor-dependent Bayesian nonparametric mixtures}
\label{subsec:clustering-theory}

A common Bayesian nonparametric approach to conditional density modeling
represents the collection $\{f(\,\cdot\mid x): x\in\mathcal{X}\}$
through predictor-indexed random probability measures. Within the
Dependent Dirichlet Process (DDP) framework of
\citet{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000}, one specifies a family
$\{G_x: x\in\mathcal{X}\}$ of random mixing measures whose dependence
across $x$ induces flexible borrowing of information in
$f(\,\cdot\mid x)$.

A generic mixture representation is
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \int k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta\right)\, dG_x(\theta),
\label{eq:clust_mix_int}
\end{equation}
where $k(\cdot\mid x,\theta)$ is a kernel and $G_x$ is a
predictor-indexed mixing measure. When $G_x$ is discrete (as under
stick-breaking constructions), latent parameters exhibit ties, inducing
a random partition of observations. Introduce latent cluster-label
variables $z_i$ and component parameters $\{\theta_j\}_{j\ge1}$ such
that
\begin{equation}
y_i \mid z_i,\{\theta_j\},x_i \sim k\!\left(\,\cdot \mid x_i,\theta_{z_i}\right).
\label{eq:clust_latent}
\end{equation}
Clustering is then characterized by the posterior distribution of the
induced partition $\Pi=\{z_1,\ldots,z_n\}$ and summarized in a
label-invariant manner using co-clustering functionals
\citep{Binder1978,Dahl2006}.

In the clustering component considered here, we allow three forms of
predictor dependence, each corresponding to a particular construction of
$G_x$. In all cases, the conditional density admits the series form
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)\, k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta_j\right),
\label{eq:clust_series}
\end{equation}
with $w_j(x)\ge0$ and $\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)=1$ for all $x$.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weight dependence only:} predictors modulate mixture
weights while atoms are shared across $x$,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)\, k\!\left(y\mid \theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_weights_only}
\end{equation}
This is appropriate when covariates change cluster prevalence but not
the within-cluster kernel shape. A common approach uses
predictor-dependent stick-breaking, where stick lengths are transformed
regressions in $x$; the logistic stick-breaking process constructs
these lengths via logistic regressions and maps them to weights
\citep{Ren2011}. Such models can be viewed as practical DDP instances in
which dependence is introduced through smoothly varying weights
\citep{MacEachern2000,Quintana2022}.

\item \textbf{Atom dependence only:} predictors enter through
component-specific kernel parameters while weights remain global,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j\, k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_param_only}
\end{equation}
Here clusters correspond to distinct regression surfaces while $w_j$
retains the interpretation of global prevalence. This structure aligns
with DDP constructions that share weights while allowing
predictor-indexed atoms \citep{MacEachern1999,MacEachern2000}, and is
closely related to ANOVA-type dependent priors for structured
collections of random measures \citep{DeIorio2004}.

\item \textbf{Weight and atom dependence:} the most flexible
specification allows predictors to influence both prevalence and
within-cluster behavior,
\begin{equation}
f(y\mid x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} w_j(x)\, k\!\left(y\mid x,\theta_j\right).
\label{eq:clust_both}
\end{equation}
This corresponds to DDP-type models in which both selection
probabilities and component-specific conditional structure vary with
predictors \citep{Quintana2022}.
\end{itemize}

Posterior clustering summaries are reported using label-invariant
functionals of $\Pi$. The posterior similarity matrix (PSM) has entries
\begin{equation}
S_{i\ell} \;=\; \Pr(z_i=z_\ell \mid \text{data}), \qquad i,\ell=1,\ldots,n,
\label{eq:psm}
\end{equation}
estimated by Monte Carlo averages over MCMC output. A representative
partition is obtained by selecting the sampled partition whose adjacency
matrix is closest to the PSM under squared loss \citep{Dahl2006},
yielding a stable point estimate while retaining the PSM as the primary
uncertainty summary. Appendix
Section~\ref{subsec:cluster_extension} provides further
details on the package's implementation of these summaries.


\subsection{Causal Inference: Notation, Identification, and Reported Estimands}
\label{subsec:causal}

We use the potential outcomes framework
\citep{Neyman1923,Rubin1974,ImbensRubin2015}. Let $A\in\{0,1\}$ denote a
binary treatment indicator ($A=1$ treated, $A=0$ control), let $X$ denote
pre-treatment covariates, and let $Y(a)$ denote the potential outcome under arm
$a\in\{0,1\}$. The observed outcome satisfies
\begin{equation}
Y = A\,Y(1) + \bigl(1-A\bigr)\,Y(0).
\label{eq:po_observed}
\end{equation}

The treatment-specific conditional distribution functions are the
fundamental inferential objects of the framework. For each arm
$a\in\{0,1\}$, the arm-specific conditional CDF is
\begin{equation}
F_a(y\mid x)=\Pr\;\{Y(a)\le y \mid X=x\},\qquad a\in\{0,1\}.
\label{eq:po_cond_cdf}
\end{equation}
In this package, the corresponding conditional densities $f_a(y\mid x)$
assume the spliced DPM structure in~\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}. The
conditional $\tau$-quantile is obtained by inverting $F_a(\cdot\mid x)$,
\begin{equation}
Q_a(\tau\mid x)=\inf\{y:\,F_a(y\mid x)\ge \tau\},\qquad \tau\in(0,1),
\label{eq:po_cond_quantile}
\end{equation}
and the conditional mean is a functional of the conditional density,
\begin{equation}
\mu_a(x)=\mathrm{E}\{Y(a)\mid X=x\}=\int y\, f_a(y\mid x)\,dy.
\label{eq:po_cond_mean}
\end{equation}

Marginal (standardized) distributions are obtained by averaging
conditional distributions over a covariate distribution. Writing
$F_a^{\mathrm{m}}$ for the population-standardized arm-$a$ CDF and
$F_a^{\mathrm{t}}$ for the treated-standardized analog, these are
defined by
\begin{equation}
F_a^{\mathrm{m}}(y)=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{F_a(y\mid X)\bigr\},
\qquad
F_a^{\mathrm{t}}(y)=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{F_a(y\mid X)\bigm\vert A=1\bigr\}.
\label{eq:marginal_cdf_defs}
\end{equation}
The associated marginal quantile functions are obtained by inverting
these standardized CDFs,
\begin{equation}
Q_a^{\mathrm{m}}(\tau)=\inf\{y:\,F_a^{\mathrm{m}}(y)\ge \tau\},
\qquad
Q_a^{\mathrm{t}}(\tau)=\inf\{y:\,F_a^{\mathrm{t}}(y)\ge \tau\},
\label{eq:marginal_quantile_defs}
\end{equation}
while the marginal means integrate the conditional mean over the
respective covariate distributions,
\begin{equation}
\mu_a^{\mathrm{m}}=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{\mu_a(X)\bigr\},
\qquad
\mu_a^{\mathrm{t}}=\mathrm{E}\bigl\{\mu_a(X)\mid A=1\bigr\}.
\label{eq:marginal_mean_defs}
\end{equation}

Identification of these quantities depends on the study design. In
randomized experiments, randomization together with positivity implies
exchangeability between treatment assignment and potential outcomes
\citep{ImbensRubin2015}. In observational studies, we rely on the
following conditions \citep{RosenbaumRubin1983,ImbensRubin2015}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA):} there is
no interference between units and no hidden versions of treatment.
\item \textbf{Consistency:} if $A=a$, then the observed outcome equals
the corresponding potential outcome, that is, $Y=Y(a)$.
\item \textbf{Conditional ignorability:} treatment assignment is
as-if randomized given covariates, so that
\begin{equation}
\bigl(Y(0),Y(1)\bigr)\ \perp\ A \ \mid\ X.
\label{eq:ignorability}
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Overlap (positivity):} every covariate profile has a
strictly positive probability of receiving either treatment, meaning
\begin{equation}
0<\Pr\;(A=1\mid X=x)<1
\label{eq:overlap}
\end{equation}
for all covariate values of interest.
\end{itemize}

Under these conditions, the treatment-specific conditional distributions
are identified from the observed data as
\begin{equation}
F_a(y\mid x)=\Pr\;(Y\le y\mid A=a,X=x),\qquad a\in\{0,1\}.
\label{eq:identification_cdf}
\end{equation}

In observational settings, the package uses the propensity score (PS) to
support the design stage \citep{RosenbaumRubin1983}. The PS is the
conditional probability of treatment assignment given covariates,
\begin{equation}
\rho(x)=\Pr\;(A=1\mid X=x),
\label{eq:ps_def}
\end{equation}
and a key property of the true propensity score is that conditioning on
it balances covariates across treatment groups. Following a two-stage
strategy, the package first estimates $\rho(x)$ using only $(A,X)$ and
then augments the outcome model covariates with the estimated score, so
that the augmented predictor vector is
\begin{equation}
r(x)=\bigl(x^{\mathsf{T}},\widehat{\rho}(x)\bigr)^{\mathsf{T}}.
\label{eq:augmented_covariate}
\end{equation}
The conditional outcome distributions are then modeled separately within
each treatment arm as functions of the augmented predictor $r(x)$, and
causal contrasts are computed as functionals of the estimated
treatment-specific distributions.
Figure~\ref{fig:causalEstimandFigure-plot} illustrates two
treatment-specific conditional densities at a representative covariate
profile. The dotted verticals mark the 90th conditional quantile for
each arm, and the double-headed arrow gives the conditional quantile
treatment effect (CQTE) at that level.

\section{Package Overview: Workflow, Model, and Inference}
\label{sec:overview}
This section briefly describes the basic workflow in using \pkg{CausalMixGPD}for conditional modeling and prediction in a one-arm setup with a generalized Pareto distribution in the tail above a covariate-dependent threshold, while the causal version of this extends the exact same modeling, estimation, and prediction process with a treatment-specific outcome model and a propensity score component. Simulated data sets provided in this package are used to demonstrate the usage of different components in this package, and these data sets are provided in \code{data()}.

\subsection{Spliced DPM--GPD model, likelihood, and priors}
\label{subsec:overview-model}

The observed data consist of $n$ outcome--covariate pairs
$\mathcal{D}=\{(y_i,x_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, where $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^p$
denotes the covariate vector for unit $i$, optionally including an
intercept column.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hlkwd{data}\hldef{(}\hlsng{"nc_posX100_p3_k2"}\hldef{,} \hlkwc{package} \hldef{=} \hlsng{"CausalMixGPD"}\hldef{)}
\hldef{overview_dat} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{data.frame}\hldef{(}\hlkwc{y} \hldef{= nc_posX100_p3_k2}\hlopt{$}\hldef{y, nc_posX100_p3_k2}\hlopt{$}\hldef{X)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

The target conditional distribution $F(\cdot\mid x)$ and its spliced
DPM--GPD construction are defined in Section~\ref{sec:prelim}, with the
spliced CDF and density given in
\eqref{eq:splice_cdf}--\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}. For posterior computation,
\pkg{CausalMixGPD} approximates the infinite bulk DPM by a finite
truncation to $J$ mixture components, so that
\begin{equation*}
f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y\mid x;\Theta)\;\approx\;\sum_{j=1}^{J} w_j\,k(y\mid x;\theta_j),
\end{equation*}
where $j=1,\ldots,J$ indexes components, each weight satisfies
$w_j\ge 0$, and the weights sum to one up to a small residual mass
assigned to the remaining components. Kernel parameters may be constant,
component-specific, or covariate-dependent through link specifications
of the form
\begin{equation}
  g\!\left(\psi_j(x)\right)=x^\top \beta_{j,\psi},
  \label{eq:overview-link}
\end{equation}
where $\psi_j(x)$ denotes a generic kernel parameter for component $j$
at covariate value $x$ and $g(\cdot)$ is a link function that enforces
the parameter support, such as a log link for positive scale parameters.
All code examples in this section use the MCMC settings shown below,
which are intentionally short for illustration; longer chains and
multiple chains are recommended for substantive analyses.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hlkwd{data.frame}\hldef{(mcmc_fixed)}
\end{alltt}
\begin{verbatim}
  niter nburnin thin nchains seed
1   250      50    1       1 2026
\end{verbatim}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}
The one-arm spliced model can be fit by inputting the formula, data, and control arguments to \code{dpmgpd()}. The \code{formula} argument follows standard \textsf{R} syntax and dictates which covariates to include in both the bulk and tail components. The  \code{backend}  argument specifies which representation of the DPM weights to use in the model. The option \code{"sb"} follows the stick breaking representation in equation (eq:sb), while the option \code{"crp"} follows the Chinese restaurant process representation. The \code{kernel} argument dictates the family of kernels used in the bulk, \code{components} dictates the truncation, and \code{mcmc} dictates the tuning.

\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}

                        \hlkwc{mcmc} \hldef{= mcmc_fixed)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

Using the spliced density mentioned in
\eqref{eq:splice_pdf}, the likelihood is
\begin{equation}
  L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})
  =\prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i\mid x_i;\Theta,\Phi),
  \label{eq:overview-lik}
\end{equation} where $\Theta$ collects the bulk
parameters $\{w_j,\theta_j\}_{j=1}^J$, and $\Phi$ collects the tail
parameters governing $u(x)$, $\sigma(x)$, and $\xi$. Equivalently, the
log-likelihood can be written as \begin{align}
  \log L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})
  \;=&\;\sum_{i=1}^n (1-\delta_i)\,\log f_{\mathrm{DP}}(y_i\mid x_i;\Theta)
  \nonumber\\
  &\;+\;\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i
  \Bigl[
    \log\!\bigl\{1-F_{\mathrm{DP}}(u(x_i)\mid x_i;\Theta)\bigr\}
    +\log f_{\mathrm{GPD}}\!\bigl(y_i\mid x_i;\Phi\bigr)
  \Bigr],
  \label{eq:overview-loglik}
\end{align} where $f_{\mathrm{GPD}}$ is the GPD tail density from
Section~\ref{subsec:splice} and
$F_{\mathrm{DP}}(\cdot\mid x)$ is the bulk mixture CDF.

If the GPD tail is not included, then we have
$f(y\mid x;\Theta, \Phi) \equiv f_{\text{DP}}(y \mid x; \Theta)$, and we
have a mixture over the bulk alone; a convenience interface to this bulk-only
model, similar to \code{dpmgpd()}, is provided by the \code{dpmix()} wrapper.
\begin{knitrout}\footnotesize
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{overview_fit} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{dpmix}\hldef{(}\hlkwc{formula} \hldef{= y} \hlopt{~} \hldef{x1} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x2} \hlopt{+} \hldef{x3,}  \hlkwc{data} \hldef{= overview_dat,}
                      \hlkwc{mcmc} \hldef{= mcmc_fixed)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

The prior distributions for the parameters $\Theta$ and $\Phi$ can be set by the user, with the defaults being weakly informative. For the main parameters of the DPM model, $\Theta$, the default prior setting is to use stick-breaking priors for the mixture weights and conjugate priors for the kernel parameters when conjugacy holds. For the parameters $\Phi$, the default setting is to use the regression-based form for the threshold $u(x)$ and scale $\sigma(x)$, together with a weakly informative prior for the shape parameter $\xi$.

\subsection{Inference and prediction}
\label{subsec:overview-mcmc}

Posterior simulation targets the joint posterior distribution of the bulk and tail parameters given data,
which is
$$p(\Theta,\Phi\mid \mathcal{D}) \propto L(\Theta,\Phi;\mathcal{D})\,p(\Theta,\Phi)$$
using the likelihood \eqref{eq:overview-lik} and the prior structure in
Section~\ref{subsec:overview-model}. \pkg{CausalMixGPD} runs MCMC
through \pkg{nimble} \citep{nimble_pkg}. The fitted object supports \code{summary()}
for posterior summaries, \code{plot()} for standard MCMC diagnostics,
and \code{params()} for a lightweight parameter extractor.

The package provides posterior summary of various functionals of the fitted conditional distribution in Section~\ref{sec:prelim}, as the estimation framework. Here in this manuscript, we focus on density $f(y\mid x)$, survival $S(y\mid x) = 1 - F(y\mid x)$, quantiles $Q(\tau\mid x)$, and mean $E(y\mid x)$. For types \code{density}, \code{survival}, and \code{quantiles}, the package evaluates each functional draw by draw from \eqref{eq:splice_cdf}--\eqref{eq:splice_quantile_case} and then summarizes the retained draws. \subsection{Causal extension and propensity score augmentation}
\label{subsec:overview-causal}

The causal framework fit separate outcome models for each treatment arm
and can optionally augment the outcome covariates with a
propensity-score summary. When propensity-score augmentation is
requiredd (Observational study), treatment assignment is modeled as
\begin{equation*}
A_i \mid \boldsymbol{x}_i \sim
\mathrm{Bernoulli}\big(e_{\boldsymbol{\gamma}}(\boldsymbol{x}_i)\big),
\end{equation*}
and the estimated propensity score is appended to the covariate vector
to form the augmented predictor
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{x})
=
\Big(\boldsymbol{x}^\top,\ \psi\big(\widehat e(\boldsymbol{x})\big)\Big)^\top,
\end{equation*}
where the PS link (logit or probit) is specified through the \code{PS}
argument and the transformation $\psi(\cdot)$ applied to the estimated
score is controlled by \code{ps\_scale} with default being identity.
The outcome model for each arm is then a spliced DPM--GPD regression on the augmented covariates,
reusing exactly the same mixture-regression and GPD-tail machinery as the one-arm workflow described in
Section~\ref{subsec:overview-model}. For
randomized experiments, the PS component is not needed and the same modeling workflow is applied to each arm without PS augmentation.

The code below loads a simulated benchmark dataset and assembles the
data frame expected by the causal wrappers, with columns for the
outcome, treatment indicator, and covariates.



The call to \code{dpmgpd.causal()} fits arm-specific spliced mixture

\section{Data analysis I: Clustering}
\label{sec:app-clustering}



This Section~\ref{sec:app-clustering} outlines the clustering process that applies to a spliced conditional mixture model. The goal is to identify latent groups among observational units, as indicated by the mixture model, and summarize clustering uncertainty in a manner that is invariant to labeling. The results will be communicated through (i) the posterior similarity matrix (PSM) and (ii) a representative partition, along with out-of-sample predictions for cluster labels.


\subsection{Data and preprocessing}

This analysis uses the \code{Boston}housing data (\pkg{MASS}) package, where each census tract is considered an observational unit. The target variable is median house value (\code{medv}), and the predictor variables are \code{lstat}, \code{rm}, and \code{nox}. The goal is to identify representative latent groups and then predict which group a new tract belongs to, using out-of-sample predictions from posterior predictive co-clustering evidence. The data is split into a training set and a test set, and the predictor variables are standardized using moments estimated from the training data. These preprocessing tasks are omitted from the code for brevity.

\section{Data analysis II: Causal Inference}
\label{sec:app-causal}

This application demonstrates causal estimands from a two-arm spliced
DPM regression with a GPD tail. In contrast to
Section~\ref{sec:app-clustering}, which targets latent partitions, the
focus here is on mean and quantile treatment contrasts,
including covariate-specific effects and upper-tail behavior.

\subsection{Data and estimands}

We use the \code{lalonde} benchmark distributed with \pkg{MatchIt} for
the Lalonde job-training application.
Here the outcome is 1978 earnings \code{re78} (USD), the treatment

\subsection{Model and fit}

Arm-specific conditional outcome distributions
$F_a(\cdot\mid \boldsymbol{x})$ are modeled via the spliced bulk--tail
construction described in Section~\ref{subsec:splice}, where
\begin{equation*}
f_a(y\mid \boldsymbol{x})
=
\sum_j w_{aj}(\boldsymbol{x})\, k(y\mid \Theta_{aj})\,\mathbf{1}(y\le u)
+
\{1-F_a(u\mid \boldsymbol{x})\}\, g(y\mid u,\sigma_a,\xi_a)\,\mathbf{1}(y>u).
\end{equation*}
The bulk kernel $k(\cdot)$ is a DPM regression
(stick-breaking backend) and the tail density $g(\cdot)$ is GPD with
scale $\sigma_a>0$ and shape $\xi_a$. The GPD tail index $\xi_a$
controls extremal heaviness (e.g., $\xi_a>0$ corresponds to heavy
tails). This model stabilizes inference for extreme quantiles by
coupling nonparametric bulk flexibility with EVT-consistent tail
extrapolation.

We fit a two-arm spliced mixture using the Gamma bulk kernel and the
same MCMC settings used throughout the manuscript. This is
not a definitive substantive analysis; the goal is to show how the
package reports standardized and profile-specific Lalonde earnings
contrasts under the spliced DPM--GPD workflow.

\subsection{Results and interpretation}

A key advantage of the spliced model is direct posterior inference on
the tail index $\xi_a$ by arm. Larger $\xi_a$ indicates a heavier upper
tail and greater propensity for extreme earnings realizations in the
corresponding arm. Table~\ref{tab:app_lalonde_tail_params} and
Table~\ref{tab:app_lalonde_tail_shape} summarize these posterior
parameter estimates.

Because both posterior arm-specific tail-shape means satisfy $\\hat\\xi_a < 1$, the corresponding arm-specific means exist; therefore the reported ATE and CATE summaries are well-defined in this analysis.

Taken together, the standardized and profile-specific summaries separate
two distinct questions: how the program shifts the overall earnings
distribution after averaging over the empirical covariate mix, and how
that shift changes across substantively different participant profiles.
The tail-shape summaries then add an explicitly extremal comparison by
showing how much posterior mass each arm assigns to heavier or lighter
upper-tail behavior.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\pkg{CausalMixGPD} combines flexible bulk density estimation, optional
EVT-based tail extrapolation, predictor-dependent clustering, and
distributional causal inference in one workflow. The same fitted model
supports posterior summaries for densities, survival curves, means,
restricted means, quantiles, and treatment contrasts, which avoids the
fragmented analysis pipelines often required when heavy tails and
distributional effects matter simultaneously.

The package is broader than the spliced examples emphasized here. Users
can work with bulk-only or spliced models, multiple kernel families on
the real line or positive support, SB or CRP backends, and clustering or
causal wrappers built on the same predictive machinery. That shared
infrastructure makes it easier to move from exploratory subgroup
analysis to tail-focused prediction and causal reporting without
changing the underlying modeling language.

The first practical limitation is computation. Mixture models with
covariate dependence, optional tail components, and arm-specific causal
fits are MCMC-intensive, and the cost increases further when users run
sensitivity analyses, longer chains, or richer predictor sets.

The second limitation is model tuning. A spliced analysis inherits
choices from both the DPM and peaks-over-threshold components, including
kernel family, truncation level, threshold specification, and the number
of exceedances available for tail learning. When overlap is weak or
exceedances are sparse, posterior uncertainty can be substantial, and
for clustering the posterior similarity matrix is often more reliable
than any single partition summary.

The third limitation is scope. The current causal implementation is
restricted to binary treatments and continuous outcomes under standard
consistency, ignorability, and overlap assumptions. Propensity-score
augmentation can improve adjustment in observational studies, but it is
not a substitute for design, overlap diagnostics, or sensitivity
analysis for unmeasured confounding.
